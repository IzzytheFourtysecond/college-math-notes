\Hstatement\blab{Set 6 Problem 5:} Let $G \subseteq \mathbb{C}$ be a simply connected region with $G \neq \mathbb{C}$.
\begin{enumerate}
	\item[(a)] Show that any holomorphic map $f: G \to G$ other than the identity admits at most one\\ fixed point.
	\begin{myIndent}\HexOne
		Suppose for the sake of contradiction that $f$ has two distinct fixed points $a, b \in G$. By\\ the Riemann mapping theorem, we know there is a biholomorphism $\theta: G \to \Delta$. In\\ turn, we know that $g \coloneqq \theta \circ f \circ \theta^{-1}$ is a holomorphic function from $\Delta$ to $\Delta$. Also,\\ $g \coloneqq \theta \circ f \circ \theta^{-1}$ has two distinct fixed points $\alpha \coloneqq \theta(a)$ and $\beta \coloneqq \theta(b)$. If we can prove that $g$ is the identity map, then we will also know that $f$ is the identity map.\retTwo

		Given any $z_0 \in \Delta$, let $\varphi_{z_0}(z)$ denote the biholomorphism of the form $\frac{z - z_0}{1 - \overline{z_0} z}$. Then note that $h \coloneqq \varphi_{\alpha} \circ g \circ \varphi_{-\alpha}$ is a holomorphism of $\Delta$ with the distinct fixed points $0$ and $\varphi_\alpha(\beta)$. By Schwarz's lemma, we can thus conclude that $h$ is a rotation. Yet the only rotation with a fixed point besides $0$ is the identity map. So, $h$ is the identity map. In turn, so is $g$.
	\end{myIndent}
	
	\item[(b)] Show that the above statement is false for $G = \mathbb{C}$.
	\begin{myIndent}\HexOne
		Let $f(z) = z^2$. Then $f$ is a holomorphic map from $\mathbb{C}$ to $\mathbb{C}$ with $0$ and $1$ as fixed points. This contradicts part (a).
	\end{myIndent}
	
	\item[(c)] Now assume $f: G \to G$ is holomorphic and admits a fixed point $a$ such that $f^\prime(a) = 1$.\\ Show that $f$ must be the identity.
	\begin{myIndent}\HexOne
		Let $\theta: G \to \Delta$ be a biholomorphic function sending $a$ to $0$. Then note that \\$g = \theta \circ f \circ \theta^{-1}$ is a holomorphic function from $\Delta$ to $\Delta$ such that $g(0) = 0$.\\ Also note that:
		
		{\centering\begin{tabular}{l}
			$g^\prime(0) = \theta^\prime(f(\theta^{-1}(0))) \cdot f^\prime(\theta^{-1}(0)) \cdot (\theta^{-1})^\prime(0)$\\ [6pt]
			$\phantom{g^\prime(0)} = \theta^\prime(a) \cdot f^\prime(a) \cdot (\theta^{-1})^\prime(0) = \theta^\prime(a) \cdot f^\prime(a) \cdot \frac{1}{\theta^\prime(\theta^{-1}(0))} = \frac{\theta^\prime(a)}{\theta^\prime(a)} \cdot f^\prime(a) = 1$
		\end{tabular}\retTwo\par}

		It follows by Schwarz' lemma that $g$ is a rotation. Also, since the only rotation with $g^\prime(0) = 1$ is the identity map, we know that $g$ is the identity map. This in turn proves that $f$ is also the identity map. $\blacksquare$
	\end{myIndent}
\end{enumerate}

	In a similar vein, note that if $F: \mcateg{D} \to \mcateg{E}$ and $G, H: \mcateg{C} \to \mcateg{D}$ are functors with\\ $\eta: G \to H$ being a natural transformation / isomorphism, then we can define a\\ natural transformation / isomorphism $\theta: F \circ G \to F \circ H$ by setting $\theta_a = F(\eta_a)$\\ for all $a \in \Obj(\mcateg{C})$.
	\begin{myIndent}\pracTwo
		A key fact I'm implicitely using here is that if $f: a \to b$ is an isomorphism then\\ $F(f): F(a) \to F(b)$ is also an isomorphism for any functor $F$ with an inverse\newpage given by $F(f^{-1})$. This is a result of the fact that functors preserve compositions of morphisms.\retTwo
	\end{myIndent}

	\item As I start to talk more about multi-argument functors (i.e. functors from products of categories), it will be more and more helpful to talk about contravariant functors $F: \mcateg{C} \to \mcateg{D}$ as instead being ordinary functors from $\mcateg{C}^\mathrm{op}$ to $\mcateg{D}$. However, there is a downside to this approach that I want to take note of.
	\begin{myIndent}\hThree
		Suppose $F: \mcateg{C} \to \mcateg{D}$ and $G: \mcateg{D} \to \mcateg{E}$ are contravariant functors. Then the codomain of $F$ and the domain of $G$ are the same and hence $G \circ F$ gives a well-defined mapping of all morphisms and objects (which we can easily check to be a functor).\retTwo

		That said, if we instead view $F$ and $G$ as ordinary functors from opposite categories, then the codomain of $F$ (which is $\mcateg{D}$) and the domain of $G$ (which is $\mcateg{D}^\mathrm{op}$) are no longer the same categories.\retTwo
	\end{myIndent}

	Fortunately, note that for any functor $F: \mcateg{C} \to \mcateg{D}$ we can define a functor\\ $\overline{F}: \mcateg{C}^\mathrm{op} \to \mcateg{D}^\mathrm{op}$ given by $\overline{F}(a) = F(a)$ and $\overline{F}(f^\mathrm{op}) = (F(f))^{\mathrm{op}}$. Then by an abuse of notation, if $G: \mcateg{D}^\mathrm{op} \to \mcateg{E}$ is another functor, we will write $G \circ F$ to denote $G \circ \overline{F}$.

	\item[1.] $B$ is an $A$-algebra if $B$ is both an $A$-module and a ring (perhaps not commutative), and satisfies that $a(m_1 m_2) = (am_1)m_2 = m_1(am_2)$ for all $a \in A$ and $m_1, m_2 \in M$.
	\item[2.] $B$ is an $A$-algebra if $B$ is a ring (perhaps not commutative) and there exists a ring\\ homomorphism $f : A \to B$ such that $f(1_A) = 1_B$ and $f(a)b = bf(a)$ for all $a \in A$\\ and $b \in B$.
\end{itemize}

\begin{myIndent}\pracTwo
	Definition 2 only makes sense if $B$ is a unital ring. That said, one can easily check that\\ definition 2 implies definition 1. After all, we can turn $B$ into an $A$ module by defining\\ $a \cdot b = f(a)b$ for all $a \in A$ and $b \in B$. Then this satisfies all requirements of definition\\ 1.\retTwo

	Meanwhile, if $B$ is unital then definition 1 implies definition 2 when we set $f(a) = a \cdot 1_B$.\retTwo
\end{myIndent}

For our coming purposes, the second definition will be more helpful.\retTwo

Suppose $B$ is a unital $A$-algebra. Then we claim that the tensor functor $T_B$ can be enriched to be a functor $A$-$\mcateg{mod} \to B$-$\mcateg{mod}$.\\ [-8pt]
\begin{myIndent}\hThree
	Suppose $M$ is an $A$-module. Then $T_B(M) = B \otimes_A M$. We define multiplication by $b$\\ (i.e. $\ell_b : B \otimes_A M \to B \otimes_A M)$ on $T_B(M)$ such that:
	
	{\center$\ell_b(b^\prime \otimes m) = bb^\prime \otimes m$.\retTwo\par}

	This is a well-defined $A$-linear map.
	\begin{myIndent}\color{orange}
		Let $\ell^\prime : B \times M \to B \otimes_A M$ be given by $(b^\prime, m) \mapsto bb^\prime \otimes m$. It is easily checked that $\ell^\prime$ is an $A$-bilinear map. In turn, by the universal property of tensor prdocuts we have that $\ell_b$ is a well-defined $A$-linear map.\newpage
	\end{myIndent}

	Now we claim that $B \otimes_A M$ is a $B$-module when for all $b \in B$ and $x \in B \otimes_A M$\\ we define $b \cdot x \coloneqq \ell_b(x)$.
	\begin{myIndent}\color{orange}
		Note from before that:
		
		{\centering$b \cdot (x_1 + x_2) = \ell_b(x_1 + x_2) = \ell_b(x_1) + \ell_b(x_2) = (b \cdot x_1) + (b \cdot x_2)$.\retTwo\par}

		As for the other three properties we need to show, it suffices to check them only for pure tensors $x = b^\prime \otimes m$ since they generate they span the entire tensor product.

		\begin{itemize}
			\item $1_B \cdot (b^\prime \otimes m) = (1_B b^\prime) \otimes m = b^\prime \otimes m$
			\item $b_1b_2 \cdot (b^\prime \otimes m) = (b_1b_2b^\prime) \otimes m = b_1 \cdot ((b_2b^\prime) \otimes m) = b_1 \cdot (b_2 \cdot (b^\prime \otimes m))$.
			\item $(b_1 + b_2) \cdot (b^\prime \otimes m) = ((b_1 + b_2)b^\prime) \otimes m$\\
			$\phantom{(b_1 + b_2) \cdot (b^\prime \otimes m)} = (b_1b^\prime \otimes m) + (b_2b^\prime \otimes m) = b_1 \cdot (b^\prime \otimes m)  + b_2 \cdot (b^\prime \otimes m)$.\retTwo
		\end{itemize}
	\end{myIndent}

	As for the arrows of the category, we claim that if $f: M \to M^\prime$ is an $A$-module\\ homomorphism then $1_B \otimes f : B \otimes_A M \to B \otimes_A M^\prime$ is a $B$-module homomorphism.
	\begin{myIndent}\color{orange}
		After all, for any pure tensor $b^\prime \otimes m \in B \otimes_A M$ we have that:

		{\centering \begin{tabular}{l}
			$b^\prime \cdot (1_B \otimes f)(b \otimes m) = b^\prime \cdot (b \otimes f(m))$\\
			$\phantom{b^\prime \cdot (1_B \otimes f)(b \otimes m)} = (b^\prime b) \otimes f(m) = (1_B \otimes f)(b^\prime b \otimes m)$\\
			$\phantom{b^\prime \cdot (1_B \otimes f)(b \otimes m) = (b^\prime b) \otimes f(m)} = (1_B \otimes f)(b^\prime \cdot ( b \otimes m)) $
		\end{tabular} \retTwo\par}
	\end{myIndent}
\end{myIndent}

On the other hand, we can define a \udefine{forgetful functor} $\mathcal{F} : B$-$\mcateg{mod} \to A$-$\mcateg{mod}$ as follows:
\begin{myIndent}\pracTwo
	Suppose $f: A \to B$ is the ring homomorphism satisfying that $f(1_A) = 1_B$ which defines the $A$-algebra structure of $B$. Then given any $B$-module $M$, define $\mathcal{F}(M)$ to be the $A$-module whose elements are the same as $M$ and in which $a \cdot m =f(a)m$ for all $a \in A$. We can then see that any $B$-linear map $\phi:M \to M^\prime$ is also $A$-linear. So, $\mathcal{F}$ is a functor.\retTwo
\end{myIndent}

\exTwo\ul{Proposition:} If $B, C$ are $A$-algebras (which aren't necessarily commutative) then $B \otimes_A C$ is also an $A$-algebra via the multiplication rule $(b_1 \otimes c_1)(b_2 \otimes c_2) = (b_1b_2 \otimes c_1c_2)$.
\begin{myIndent}\exThreeP
	Firstly, we need to show this multiplication rule is well-defined. Suppose\\ $x = \sum_{i=1}^k a_i(b_i \otimes c_i)$ is an element of $B \otimes_A C$ and define:

	{\centering $\ell^\prime : B \times C \to B \otimes_A C$ by $\ell^\prime(b, c) = \sum_{i=1}^k a_i(b_i b \otimes c_i c)$. \retTwo\par}

	$\ell^\prime$ is clearly an $A$-bilinear map. Hence, by the universal property of the tensor product\\ there is a unique $A$-linear map $\ell_x : B \otimes_A C \to B \otimes_A C$ with:
	
	{\centering$\ell_x(b \otimes c) = \sum_{i=1}^k a_i(b_i b \otimes c_i c)$.\retTwo\par}

	Now to finish proving well-definedness, we need to show that if $\sum_{j=1}^m a_j^\prime(b_j^\prime \otimes c_j^\prime)$ also\\ [-3pt] equals $x$ then we will have that $\widehat{\ell}_x = \ell_x$ where $\widehat{\ell}_x : B \otimes_A C \to B \otimes_A C$ is the unique $A$-linear map satisfying that $\widehat{\ell}_x(b \otimes c) = \sum_{j=1}^m a_j^\prime (b_j^\prime b \otimes c_j^\prime c)$.

	\begin{myIndent}\exPPP
		\begin{myIndent}\color{RawerSienna}
			Note that $\widehat{\ell}_x$ is well-defined by the same reasoning that $\ell_x$ is well-defined.\retTwo
		\end{myIndent}

		Firstly, note that we equivalently need to show that $\ell_x - \widehat{\ell}_x$ is the zero map. And since pure tensors span all of $B \otimes_A C$, it thus suffices to prove that $(\ell_x - \widehat{\ell}_x)(b \otimes c) = 0$ for all $b \in B$ and $c \in C$.\newpage

		Fortunately, by analogous reasoning to before we can see that $\ell^{b \otimes c}$ is also a well-\\defined $A$-linear map from $B \otimes_A C$ to $B \otimes_A C$ where $\ell^{b \otimes c}(b^\prime \otimes c^\prime) = b^\prime b \otimes c^\prime c$. Also, we can see that:

		{\centering $(\ell_x - \widehat{\ell}_x)(b \otimes c) = \ell^{b \otimes c}((\sum_{i=1}^k a_i(b_i \otimes c_i)) - (\sum_{j=1}^m a_j^\prime(b_j^\prime \otimes c_j^\prime)))$ \retTwo\par}

		Therefore as $(\sum_{i=1}^k (b_i \otimes c_i)) - (\sum_{j=1}^m (b_j^\prime \otimes c_j^\prime)) = 0$ we can conclude that:

		{\centering$(\ell_x - \widehat{\ell}_x)(b \otimes c) = \ell_{b \otimes c}(0) = 0$.\retTwo\par}
	\end{myIndent}

	And with that we've successfully proven that for any $x, y \in B \otimes_A C$ the value of $\ell_x(y)$\\ is independent of how we write $x$ and $y$. So, we shall define multiplication on $B \otimes_A C$ as\\ $x \cdot y = \ell_x(y)$.\retTwo

	\mySepThree

	Next, we show that our multiplication operation on $B \otimes_A C$ satisfies all the algebraic\\ properties it needs to.
	\begin{itemize}\exPPP
		\item Firstly, note that since $\ell_x$ is an $A$ linear map, we have for all $x,y_1,y_2 \in B \otimes_A C$\\ and $a_1, a_2 \in A$ that:
		
		{\centering$x(a_1y_1 + a_2y_2) = \ell_x(a_1y_1 + a_2y_2) = a_1\ell_x(y_1) + a_2\ell_x(y_2) = a_1(xy_1) + a_2(xy_2)$.\retTwo\par}

		\item Also note from the definition of $\ell_{x_i}$ that $a_1\ell_{x_1} + a_2\ell_{x_2} = \ell_{a_1x_1 + a_2x_2}$. Therefore, we can similarly conclude $(a_1x_1 + a_2x_2)y = a_1(x_1y) + a_2(x_2 y)$ for all $x_1, x_2, y \in B \otimes_A C$ and $a_1, a_2 \in A$.\retTwo
		
		\item Suppose $x = \sum_{i=1}^m a_i(b_i \otimes c_i)$, $y = \sum_{j=1}^n a_j^\prime(b_j^\prime \otimes c_j^\prime)$, and $z = \sum_{k=1}^r a_k^\pprime( b_k^\pprime \otimes c_k^\pprime)$. Then:\\ [-18pt]
		
		{\centering\begin{tabular}{l}
			$x \cdot (y \cdot z) = x \cdot\left(\sum\limits_{k=1}^r a_k^\pprime (y \cdot (b_k^\pprime \otimes c_k^\pprime))\right) = x \cdot \left(\sum\limits_{k=1}^r a_k^\pprime (\sum\limits_{j=1}^n a_j^\prime (b_j^\prime b_k^\pprime \otimes c_j^\prime c_k^\pprime))\right)$\\ [12pt]
			$\phantom{x \cdot (y \cdot z) = x \cdot\left(\sum\limits_{k=1}^r a_k^\pprime (y \cdot (b_k^\pprime \otimes c_k^\pprime))\right)} = \sum\limits_{j=1}^n \sum\limits\limits_{k=1}^r a_j^\prime a_k^\pprime (x \cdot (b_j^\prime b_k^\pprime \otimes c_j^\prime c_k^\pprime))$\\ [12pt]
			$\phantom{x \cdot (y \cdot z) = x \cdot\left(\sum\limits_{k=1}^r a_k^\pprime (y \cdot (b_k^\pprime \otimes c_k^\pprime))\right)} = \sum\limits_{j=1}^n \sum\limits\limits_{k=1}^r a_j^\prime a_k^\pprime (\sum\limits_{i=1}^m a_i(b_i b_j^\prime b_k^\pprime \otimes c_i c_j^\prime c_k^\pprime))$\\ [12pt]
			$\phantom{x \cdot (y \cdot z) = x \cdot\left(\sum\limits_{k=1}^r a_k^\pprime (y \cdot (b_k^\pprime \otimes c_k^\pprime))\right)} = \sum\limits_{i=1}^m\sum\limits_{j=1}^n \sum\limits\limits_{k=1}^r a_i a_j^\prime a_k^\pprime (b_i b_j^\prime b_k^\pprime \otimes c_i c_j^\prime c_k^\pprime)$
		\end{tabular} \retTwo\par}

		Similarly, we have that:

		{\centering\begin{tabular}{l}
			$(x \cdot y) \cdot z = \left(\sum\limits_{j=1}^n a_j^\prime(x \cdot (b_j^\prime \otimes c_j^\prime))\right) \cdot z$\\ [12pt]
			$\phantom{(x \cdot y) \cdot z} = \left(\sum\limits_{j=1}^n a_j^\prime(\sum\limits_{i=1}^m a_i(b_i b_j^\prime \otimes c_i c_j^\prime))\right) \cdot z$\\ [12pt]
			$\phantom{(x \cdot y) \cdot z} = \sum\limits_{k=1}^r a_k^\pprime \left(\sum\limits_{i=1}^m \sum\limits_{j=1}^n a_i a_j^\prime (b_i b_j^\prime \otimes c_i c_j^\prime)\right) \cdot (b_k^\pprime \otimes c_k^\pprime)$\\ [12pt]
			$\phantom{(x \cdot y) \cdot z} = \sum\limits_{k=1}^r a_k^\pprime \sum\limits_{i=1}^m \sum\limits_{j=1}^n a_i a_j^\prime (b_i b_j^\prime b_k^\pprime \otimes c_i c_j^\prime c_k^\pprime) = \sum\limits_{i=1}^m\sum\limits_{j=1}^n \sum\limits\limits_{k=1}^r a_i a_j^\prime a_k^\pprime (b_i b_j^\prime b_k^\pprime \otimes c_i c_j^\prime c_k^\pprime)$.
		\end{tabular}\newpage\par}

		Thus $x(yz) = (xy)z$ for all $x, y, z \in B \otimes_A C$. $\blacksquare$\retTwo
	\end{itemize}

\exTwo\ul{Theorem:} Suppose $B$ is a commutative unital $A$-algebra whose algebra structure is defined by the group homomorphism $f: A \to B$. If $\mathfrak{a} \lhd A[x]$ then $\frac{A[x]}{\mathfrak{a}} \otimes_A  B \cong \frac{B[x]}{\mathfrak{a}^e}$ as\\ $B$-algebras where $\mathfrak{a}^e = \langle f(\mathfrak{a})\rangle$.\\ [-10pt]
\begin{myIndent}\myComment
	$\mathfrak{a}^e$ is called the \udefine{extension} of $\mathfrak{a}$ under $f$. There are two notes I need to give for this definition of $\mathfrak{a}^e$.
	\begin{itemize}
		\item[1.] Given a ring homomorphism $f: A \to B$, there is a unique ring homomorphism\\ $\widetilde{f}: A[x] \to B[x]$ such that $\widetilde{f}(a) = f(a)$ for all constants $a$ and $\widetilde{f}(x) = x$. Namely, this homomorphism is given by applying $f$ coefficient-wise to any polynomial. A\\ [0pt] common abuse of notation (which I used above) is to just write $f$ instead of $\widetilde{f}$. Also,\\ we will often write $\widetilde{f}(p(x))$ as $p^f(x)$.
		\item[2.] An image of an ideal under a ring homomorphism isn't guarenteed to be an ideal unless the homomorphism is surjective. (This is easy to check). That's why when definining the extension of $\mathfrak{a}$ we need to take the ideal generated by $f(\mathfrak{a})$.\retTwo
	\end{itemize}

	\exThreeP Proof:\\
	Define $\theta: \frac{A[x]}{\mathfrak{a}} \times B \to \frac{B[x]}{\mathfrak{a}^e}$ by $\left((\sum a_i x^i) + \mathfrak{a}, b\right) \mapsto \sum bf(a_i) x^i + \mathfrak{a}^e$.

	\begin{myIndent}\exPPP
		To show this is well-defined, suppose $(\sum a_i x^i) \equiv (\sum a_j^\prime x^j) \mMod{\mathfrak{a}}$. Then\\ $(\sum a_i x^i) - (\sum a_j^\prime x^j) \in \mathfrak{a}$, which in turn implies $(\sum f(a_i) x^i) - (\sum f(a_j^\prime) x^j) \in \mathfrak{a}^e$.\\ And finally this means that $\sum b f(a_i) x^i \equiv \sum b f(a_j^\prime) x^j \mMod{\mathfrak{a}^e}$.\retTwo
	\end{myIndent}

	It's obvious that $\theta$ is $A$-bilinear. Hence by the universal property of the tensor product, we know there is a unique $A$-module homomorphism $\widehat{\theta}: \frac{A[x]}{\mathfrak{a}} \times B \to \frac{B[x]}{\mathfrak{a}^e}$ such that $\widehat{\theta}(\overline{p(x)} \otimes b) = \overline{b p^f(x)}$.
	\begin{myTindent}\color{RawerSienna}
		(Here $\overline{p(x)}$ is just denoting the coset containing $p(x)$.)\retTwo
	\end{myTindent}

	Conversely, define $\psi: B[x] \to \frac{A[x]}{\mathfrak{a}} \otimes_A B$ by $\psi(\sum b_i x^i) = \sum (\overline{x^i} \otimes b_i)$. It is easy to check that $\psi$ is a $B$-algebra homomorphism. Also note that if $\sum a_i x^i \in \mathfrak{a}$, then using the $A$-balance property we get that:

	{\centering $\psi(\sum f(a_i)x^i) = \sum (\overline{x^i} \otimes f(a_i)) = \sum (\overline{ax^i} \otimes 1_B) = ((\sum a_i x^i) + \mathfrak{a}) \otimes 1_B = 0$ \retTwo\par}

	Therefore $f(\mathfrak{a}) \subseteq \ker(\psi)$. In turn, this also proves $\mathfrak{a}^e \subseteq \ker(\psi)$. Hence, by quotienting out $\mathfrak{a}^e$, we get a well-defined $B$-algebra homomorphism $\widehat{\psi}: \frac{B[x]}{\mathfrak{a}^e} \to \frac{A[x]}{\mathfrak{a}} \otimes_A B$.\newpage
	
	To finish off, we just need to show that $\widehat{\theta}$ and $\widehat{\psi}$ are inverses of each other. We do this by the same methods we have done in every other proof.\retTwo

	Also note that because inverses of $B$-algebra homomorphisms are themselves $B$-algebra homomorphisms, we have that $\widehat{\theta}$ is a $B$-algebra homomorphism. $\blacksquare$.\retTwo
\end{myIndent}

\blab{Set 8 Problem 8:} suppose $\alpha, \beta \in \mathbb{C}$ are algebraic over $\mathbb{Q}$. Let $f \coloneqq m_{\alpha; \mathbb{Q}}$ and $g \coloneqq m_{\beta; \mathbb{Q}}$. \begin{myIndent}\HexOne
	To start off, we show that $f$ is irreducible in $(\mathbb{Q}[\beta])[x]$ iff $[\mathbb{Q}[\alpha, \beta] : \mathbb{Q}] = \deg(f)\deg(g)$.

	\begin{myIndent}\HexTwoP
		Note that if $f$ is irreducible in $(\mathbb{Q}[\beta])[x]$, then as $f(\alpha) = 0$ and $f$ is monic we\\ have that $f = m_{\alpha; \mathbb{Q}[\beta]}$. Hence by the tower formula we can conclude that:

		{\centering $[\mathbb{Q}[\alpha, \beta] : \mathbb{Q}] = [\mathbb{Q}[\alpha, \beta], \mathbb{Q}[\beta]] \cdot [\mathbb{Q}[\beta] : \mathbb{Q}] = \deg(f)\deg(g)$. \retTwo\par}

		Conversely, suppose $[\mathbb{Q}[\alpha, \beta] : \mathbb{Q}] = \deg(f)\deg(g)$. Then by the tower formula we can guarentee that $\deg(m_{\alpha; \mathbb{Q}[\beta]}) = \frac{[\mathbb{Q}[\alpha, \beta] : \mathbb{Q}]}{\deg(g)} = \deg(f)$. Since $m_{\alpha ; \mathbb{Q}[\beta]}$ divides $f$ (as both evaluate to $0$ at $\alpha$) and both polynomials are monic, we can conclude that $f = m_{\alpha ; \mathbb{Q}[\beta]}$. And this proves that $f$ is irreducible in $\mathbb{Q}[\beta]$.\retTwo
	\end{myIndent}

	By symmetric reasoning, we have that $g$ is irreducible in $(\mathbb{Q}[\alpha])[x]$ iff\\ $[\mathbb{Q}[\alpha, \beta] : \mathbb{Q}] = \deg(f)\deg(g)$. And this let's us conclude what we wanted. $\blacksquare$\retTwo
\end{myIndent}

\blab{Set 8 Problem 7:} Prove that $\mathbb{Q}[\sqrt{2}]$ and $\mathbb{Q}[\sqrt{3}]$ are not isomorphic (as fields).
\begin{myIndent}\HexOne
	Suppose for the sake of contradiction that $\theta: \mathbb{Q}[\sqrt{2}] \to \mathbb{Q}[\sqrt{3}]$ is a field isomorphism, then note that since $\theta(1) = 1$ and $\theta$ preserves sums and differences, we can conclude that $\theta(n) = n$ for all $n \in \mathbb{Z}$. In turn, since $\theta$ preserves division we must have that $\theta(q) = q$ for all $q \in \mathbb{Q}$.\retTwo

	Next, consider that $\theta(\sqrt{2})^2 = \theta(2) = 2$. Yet simultaneously since the minimal polynomial of $\sqrt{3}$ over $\mathbb{Q}$ is $x^2 - 3$, we know that $\{1, \sqrt{3}\}$ is a $\mathbb{Q}$-basis of $\mathbb{Q}[\sqrt{3}]$ and in turn that\\ $\theta(\sqrt{2}) = p + q\sqrt{3}$ for some $p, q \in \mathbb{Q}$. So, we can conclude that:

	{\centering $p^2 + 2pq\sqrt{3} + 3q^2 = 2$ \retTwo\par}

	Equivalently, this means that $(p^2 + 3q^2 - 2) + (2pq)\sqrt{3} = 0$. And since $1$ and $\sqrt{3}$ are $\mathbb{Q}$-linearly independent, we know $p^2 + 3q^2 - 2 = 0$ and $2pq = 0$. But now this implies that either $p = 0$ or $q = 0$. We can check as follows that both of these possibilities lead to a contradiction. 
	\begin{itemize}
		\item If $q = 0$ then we must have that $p^2 = 2$. Yet this is impossible as no $p \in \mathbb{Q}$ satisfies that $p^2 = 2$.
		\item If $p = 0$ then we have that $q^2 = \frac{2}{3}$ Yet once again no $q \in \mathbb{Q}$ satisfies this.
	\end{itemize}

	Thus we conclude no such $\theta$ can exist. $\blacksquare$\retTwo
\end{myIndent}

As $o(a) \neq 1$ since $a \neq 1$, this proves that $o(a) = 3$.\\ [2pt] Thus $3 \divides p - 1$. Or in other words, $p \equiv 1 \mMod{3}$.\retTwo

			\item Conversely, suppose $p \equiv 1 \mMod{3}$. Then we know that $3 \divides p - 1 = |(\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z})^\times|$. Also note that because $(\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z})^\times$ is a cyclic group, there exists $b \in (\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z})^\times$ with $o(b) = p - 1$. Now just let $a \coloneqq b^{(p-1)/3}$. Then we have that $a^3 \equiv 1 \mMod{p}$ but $a \not\equiv 1 \mMod{p}$. It follows that $a$ is a root of $x^2 + x + 1$ instead of $x - 1$ and this proves that $x^2 + x + 1$ is not irreducible.

			\begin{myIndent}\HexTwoP
				As a side note, in case that $p \equiv 1 \mMod{3}$, then $a_1 = b^{(p-1)/3}$ and\\ $a_2 = b^{2(p-1)/3}$ would be two distinct zeros of $x^2 + x + 1$. Hence, we've proven that if $p \neq 3$ and  $\langle x^2 + x + 1\rangle$ is not irreducible, then there are distinct $a_1, a_2 \in \mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}$ with $x^2 + x + 1 = (x - a_1)(x - a_2)$.
			\end{myIndent}
		\end{itemize}

	\end{myIndent}
\end{enumerate}
