\pracOne One other note I want to make is that if $\langle x_\alpha\rangle_{\alpha \in A}$ is an ultra-net and $X = \bigcup_{i=1}^n E_i$, then\\ we can conclude that there is at least one $E_i$ such that $x_\alpha$ is eventually in $E_i$.
\begin{myIndent}\pracTwo
	Proof:\\
	We proceed by induction on $n$. For our base case, suppose $X = E_1 \cup E_2$. Then if $x_\alpha$ is not eventually in $E_1$, we know that $x_\alpha$ is eventually in $X - E_1$. And since $X - E_1 \subseteq E_2$, we can conclude that $x_\alpha$ is eventually in $E_2$.\retTwo

	Now suppose $n > 2$ and that $X = \bigcup_{i=1}^n E_i$. Then by writing:
	
	{\centering$X = (E_1 \cup E_2) \cup E_3 \cup \cdots \cup E_n$,\\ [4pt]\par}
	
	we can conclude by induction that either there exists $i \geq 3$ with $x_\alpha$ eventually in $E_i$, or\\ we have that $x_\alpha$ is eventually in $E_1 \cup E_2$. We're trivially done in the former case. So assume the latter case is what happens.\retTwo

	Next, write $X = E_1 \cup (E_2 \cup E_3) \cup E_4 \cup \cdots \cup E_n$. Then we know from our induction hypothesis that either there exists $i \neq 2, 3$ with $x_\alpha$ eventually in $E_i$ or we have that $x_\alpha$ is eventually in $E_2 \cup E_3$. We assume the former case doesn't happen since otherwise we'd be trivially done. In particular, this means that $x_\alpha$ is not eventually in $E_1$.\retTwo

	But now we have that $x_\alpha$ is eventually in $(E_1 \cup E_2)$, and yet at the same time since $\langle x_\alpha\rangle_{\alpha \in A}$ is an ultrafilter, we can conclude that $x_\alpha$ is eventually in $X - E_1$. In turn, we can conclude that $x_\alpha$ is eventually in $(E_1 \cup E_2) \cap (X - E_1) = E_2 - E_1 \subseteq E_2$. $\blacksquare$\retTwo
\end{myIndent}

\exTwo\ul{Proposition:} Suppose $X$ is a uniform space. Then $X$ is totally bounded if and only if every net $\langle x_\alpha\rangle_{\alpha \in A}$ in $X$ admits a Cauchy subnet.
\begin{myIndent}\exThreeP
	$(\Longrightarrow)$\\
	Let $\langle x_{\alpha_\beta}\rangle_{\beta \in B}$ be an ultra-subnet of $\langle x_\alpha\rangle_{\alpha \in A}$. Then I claim that $\langle x_{\alpha_\beta}\rangle_{\beta \in B}$ is Cauchy since more generally, any ultra-net $\langle y_\gamma\rangle_{\gamma \in C}$ is Cauchy in a totally bounded space.\\ [-6pt]
	\begin{myIndent}\exPPP
		To see why, let $\{y_\gamma\}_{\gamma \in C}$ be an ultra-net. Then suppose $E$ is any entourage of $X$. Firstly, we find an entourage $E^\prime$ such that $E^\prime = (E^\prime)^{-1}$ and $E^\prime \circ E^\prime \subseteq E$. Next,\\ note that since $X$ is totally bounded, there exists $x_1, \ldots, x_n \in X$ such that\\ $X = \bigcup_{i=1}^n E^\prime[x_i]$. Since $\{y_\gamma\}_{\gamma \in C}$ is an ultra-net, we know there must exist some\\ $x_i$ such that $y_\gamma$ is eventually in $E^\prime[x_i]$.\retTwo

		But now let $\gamma_0 \in C$ be such that $y_\gamma \in E^\prime[x_i]$ for all $\gamma \geq \gamma_0$. Then for all\\ $\gamma_1, \gamma_2 \geq \gamma_0$ in $C$ we have that $x_i E^\prime y_{\gamma_1}$ and $x_i E^\prime y_{\gamma_2}$. This implies that $y_{\gamma_1} E y_{\gamma_2}$.\retTwo
	\end{myIndent}

	$(\Longleftarrow)$\\
	Suppose that $X$ is not a totally bounded space. Thus, we know there exists an entourage $E$ such that $E[S]$ is a proper subset of $X$ for all finite subsets $S$ of $X$.\retTwo

	Let $\mathcal{S}$ be the collection of all finite subsets of $X$. This becomes a directed set when\\ partially ordered by inclusion. Next, for every $S \in \mathcal{S}$ let $x_S$ be an element of $X - E[S]$. Then, we can consider the net $\langle x_S\rangle_{S \in \mathcal{S}}$.\newpage

	I claim that $\langle x_S\rangle_{S \in \mathcal{S}}$ has no Cauchy subnets. To see why, suppose that $\langle x_{S_\beta}\rangle_{\beta \in B}$ is any subnet of $\langle x_S\rangle_{S \in \mathcal{S}}$. Then consider any fixed $\beta_0 \in B$. It suffices to show that there exists\\ [2pt] $\beta_1, \beta_2 \geq \beta_0$ in $B$ such that $(x_{S_{\beta_1}}, x_{S_{\beta_2}}) \notin E$. Fortunately, just let $\beta_1 = \beta_0$ and then let $\beta_2 \in B$ be such that $\beta_2 \geq \beta_0$ and $S_{\beta_2} \supseteq S_{\beta_0} \cup \{x_{S_{\beta_0}}\}$. Then we know $(x_{S_{\beta_1}}, x_{S_{\beta_2}}) \notin E$. $\blacksquare$\retTwo
\end{myIndent}

\hTwo Before wrapping up for the day, I also want to translate the prior result into a result about Cauchy sequences. I think this is especially worth doing since the constructions in this simpler case are less set-theoretic.\retTwo

\exTwo\ul{Proposition:} Suppose $X$ is a uniform space whose uniform structure is generated by a countable filterbase $\{E_k\}_{k \in \mathbb{N}}$. Then $X$ being totally bounded implies every sequence $\{x_n\}_{n \in \mathbb{N}}$ in $X$ admits a Cauchy subsequence.
\begin{myIndent}\exThreeP
	Proof:\\
	For each $k$ we can pick $E_k^\prime$ such that $E_k^\prime = (E_k^\prime)^{-1}$ and $E_k^\prime \circ E_k^\prime \subseteq E_k$. Then because $X$ is totally bounded, we know for each $k$ that there exists $y_1^{(k)}, \ldots, y_{m(k)}^{(k)} \in X$ such that $X = \bigcup_{j=1}^{m(k)} E^\prime_k[y_j^{(k)}]$.\retTwo
	
	But now suppose $\{x_n\}_{n \in \mathbb{N}}$ is any sequence in $X$. Then there exists some $y_j^{(1)}$ as well as some subsequence $\{x_{n_i^{(1)}}\}_{i \in \mathbb{N}}$ such that $x_{n_i^{(1)}} \in E^\prime_1[y_j^{(1)}]$ for all $i$. Next, there exists a\\ [-2pt] subsequence $\{x_{n_i^{(2)}}\}_{i \in \mathbb{N}}$ of $\{x_{n_i^{(1)}}\}_{i \in \mathbb{N}}$ as well as some $y_j^{(2)}$ with $x_{n_i^{(2)}} \in E^\prime_2[y_j^{(2)}]$ for all $i$. Continuing on in this manner, for every $k \in \mathbb{N}$ we can find a sequence $\{x_{n_i^{(k)}}\}_{i \in \mathbb{N}}$ which is a subsequence of $\{x_{n_i^{(k^\prime)}}\}_{i \in \mathbb{N}}$ for all $k^\prime < k$ and which also satisfies that there exists $y^{(k)} \in X$ with $x_{n_i^{(k)}} \in E_k^\prime[y^{(k)}]$ for all $i$.\retTwo

	To finish off, we now do a diagonal argument on all of the previous sequences we\\ constructed. Namely, consider the sequence $\{z_k\}_{k \in \mathbb{N}}$ where $z_k \coloneqq x_{n_k^{(k)}}$. This is a\\ [-4pt] subsequence of $\{x_n\}_{n \in \mathbb{N}}$. Also I claim it is Cauchy. 
	\begin{myIndent}\exPPP 
		After all, suppose $D$ is an entourage of $X$. Then we can find an entourage $E_k$ such that $E_k \subseteq D$. Next, note for all $k_1, k_2 \geq k$ that $y^{(k)} E_k^\prime z_{k_1}$ and $y^{(k)} E_k^\prime z_{k_2}$. Hence, we know that $z_{k_1} E_k z_{k_2}$. So, $(z_{k_1}, z_{k_2}) \in E_k \subseteq D$ for all $k_1, k_2 \geq k$.  \exThreeP $\blacksquare$\retTwo
	\end{myIndent}
\end{myIndent}

\ul{Proposition:} Suppose $X$ is a uniform space. If every sequence $\{x_n\}_{n \in \mathbb{N}}$ in $X$ admits a Cauchy subsequence then $X$ is totally bounded.
\begin{myIndent}\exThreeP
	Proof:\\
	Suppose $X$ is not totally bounded and let $E$ be an entourage such that $E[S] \neq X$ for all finite subsets $S$ of $X$. By shrinking $E$, we can without loss of generality assume that $E$ is symmetric (i.e. $E = E^{-1}$). Now pick any $x_1 \in X$. We can find $x_2 \in X - E[x_1]$. Next, we can find $x_3 \in X- E[x_1, x_2]$. And continuing on in this fashion, we can find $x_n \in X - \bigcup_{i=1}^{n-1} E[x_i]$. Then we consider the sequence $\{x_n\}_{n \in \mathbb{N}}$.\retTwo

	Trivially, no subsequence (or even any subnet I now realize) of $\{x_n\}_{n \in \mathbb{N}}$ will be Cauchy since $(x_{n_1}, x_{n_2}) \notin E$ whenever $n_1 \neq n_2$. $\blacksquare$
\end{myIndent}

Today I want to start tying the theory of uniform spaces to the theory of topology. So, I'll continue loosely following the book I was following before.\retTwo

Let $(X, \mathscr{U})$ be a uniform space. Then taking inspiration from \inLinkRap{Page 798 theorem 4-3-5}{pages 798-800} as well as from how the metric space topology is defined, we'll define that a set $U \subseteq X$ is open in the uniform topology if for all $x \in U$ there exists an entourage $E \in \mathscr{U}$ with $E[x] \subseteq U$.\retTwo

\exTwo\ul{Proposition:} The uniform topology is a well-defined topology. 
\begin{myIndent}\exThreeP
	Proof:\\
	To start off, we trivially have that $\emptyset$ and $X$ are open sets in the uniform topology.\retTwo

	Next suppose $U_1$ and $U_2$ are open sets in the uniform topology, and consider any\\ $x \in U_1 \cap U_2$. Since $U_1$ and $U_2$ are open, we can find entourages $E_1$ and $E_2$ such that $E_1[x] \subseteq U_1$ and $E_2[x] \subseteq U_2$. So, we then consider the entourage $E \coloneqq E_1 \cap E_2$. Now $E[x] \subseteq U_1 \cap U_2$. And since $x$ was arbitrary, we've proven that $U_1 \cap U_2$ is an open set in the uniform topology.
	\begin{myIndent}\exPPP
		(By induction we can in turn conclude that any finite intersection of open sets is open in the uniform topology.)\retTwo
	\end{myIndent}

	Finally, suppose $\{U_\alpha\}_{\alpha \in A}$ is an arbitrary collection of open sets in the uniform topology. Then for any $x \in \bigcup_{\alpha \in A}U_\alpha$ we know that there exists $\alpha_0 \in A$ with $x \in U_{\alpha_0}$. Next, since $U_{\alpha_0}$ is open we know that there exist an entourage $E$ such that $E[x] \subseteq U_{\alpha_0}$. And now $E[x] \subseteq \bigcup_{\alpha \in A}U_\alpha$. This proves that $\bigcup_{\alpha \in A}U_\alpha$ is open. $\blacksquare$\retTwo
\end{myIndent}

\exTwo\ul{Proposition:} Suppose $\mathscr{B}$ is a filterbase for $\mathscr{U}$. Then for any $x \in X$ we have that\\ $\{E[x] : E \in \mathscr{B}\}$ is a local base of neighborhoods of $x$. {\color{red} (See \inLinkRap{local base of neighborhoods definition}{page 796} for the definition\\ of a local base of neighborhoods.)}
\begin{myIndent}\exThreeP
	Proof:\\
	To start off, suppose $N$ is any neighborhood of $x$. Then we know there is an open set $U$ such that $x \in U \subseteq N$. But now since $U$ is open, we know there exists some entourage $D \in \mathscr{U}$ such that $D[x] \subseteq U$. And finally, since $\mathscr{B}$ is a filterbase for $\mathscr{U}$ we know that there exists another entourage $E \in \mathscr{B}$ with $E \subseteq D$. In turn, $E[x] \subseteq D[x]$. And with that, we've proven that for all neighborhoods $N$ of $x$ there exists $E \in \mathscr{B}$ with $x \in E[x] \subseteq N$.\retTwo
	
	Meanwhile, we also need to show that $E[x]$ is a neighborhood of $x$ for all $E \in \mathscr{B}$. So, having fixed our entourage $E$, let $U \coloneqq \{y \in X : \exists D \in \mathscr{U} \text{ s.t. } D[y] \subseteq E[x]\}$. Note that:
	\begin{itemize}
		\item $x \in U$ since $E \in \mathscr{U}$ and $E[x] \subseteq E[x]$.
		\item Also since $y \in D[y]$ for all entourages $D \in \mathscr{U}$ and $y \in X$, we know that $U \subseteq E[x]$.
		\item Finally, $U$ is open. After all, consider any $y \in U$ and let $D \in \mathscr{B}$ be such that\\ $D[y] \subseteq E[x]$. But now since $\mathscr{U}$ is a uniform structure, we can find some $D^\prime \in \mathscr{U}$\\ such that $D^\prime \circ D^\prime \subseteq D$. And now I claim that $D^\prime[y] \subseteq U$.
		\begin{myIndent}\exPPP
			After all, suppose $z \in D^\prime[y]$. Then for any $w \in D^\prime[z]$ we have that $y D^\prime z$ and $z D^\prime w$. It follows that $y D^\prime \circ D^\prime w$. So, $w \in D[y] \subseteq E[x]$. And this proves that\newpage $D^\prime[z] \subseteq E[x]$. In turn, by the definition of $U$ we know that $z \in U$. And since $z$ was arbitrary, this proves that $D^\prime[y] \subseteq U$.\retTwo
		\end{myIndent}
	\end{itemize}

\exTwo\ul{Corollary:} If $(X, \rho)$ is a metric space, then the uniform topology associated with the metric uniform structure is precisely the standard metric topology.
\begin{myIndent}\exThreeP
	Proof:\\
	Let $\{D_\varepsilon\}_{\varepsilon > 0}$ be the standard uniformity base for the metric uniform structure. Then we can see for all $x \in X$ that $D_\varepsilon[x]$ is just the ball of radius $\varepsilon$ centered at $x$. Hence, for any $x \in X$ we have that $\{D_\varepsilon[x], \varepsilon > 0\}$ is a local base of neighborhoods of $x$ in both the standard metric topology and in the uniform topology. It follows that convergence in both topologies are equivalent, and in turn the topologies are equal to each other. $\blacksquare$\retTwo 
\end{myIndent}

\ul{Corollary 2:} Let $G$ be a topological group. Then the uniform topologies associated with the left uniform structure and the right uniform structure are both equivalent to the original topology on $G$.
\begin{myIndent}\exThreeP
	Proof:\\
	Just show like in the prior corollary that either of the uniform topologies on $G$ admits\\ a local base of neighborhoods at any $x \in G$ which is also a local base of neighborhoods\\ of $x$ in the original topology. \end{myIndent}

\exTwo\ul{Proposition:} Suppose $X$ and $Y$ are uniform spaces and $\phi: X \to Y$ is uniformly continuous. Then $\phi$ is continuous when $X$ and $Y$ are equipped with the uniform topologies.
\begin{myIndent}\exThreeP
	Proof:\\

This proves that $\phi$ is continuous\\ at $x$. And since $x$ was arbitrary, we can conclude that $\phi$ is continuous everywhere. $\blacksquare$\retTwo
\end{myIndent}
