\Hstatement\blab{Set 6 Problem 5:} Let $G \subseteq \mathbb{C}$ be a simply connected region with $G \neq \mathbb{C}$.
\begin{enumerate}
	\item[(a)] Show that any holomorphic map $f: G \to G$ other than the identity admits at most one\\ fixed point.
	\begin{myIndent}\HexOne
		Suppose for the sake of contradiction that $f$ has two distinct fixed points $a, b \in G$. By\\ the Riemann mapping theorem, we know there is a biholomorphism $\theta: G \to \Delta$. In\\ turn, we know that $g \coloneqq \theta \circ f \circ \theta^{-1}$ is a holomorphic function from $\Delta$ to $\Delta$. Also,\\ $g \coloneqq \theta \circ f \circ \theta^{-1}$ has two distinct fixed points $\alpha \coloneqq \theta(a)$ and $\beta \coloneqq \theta(b)$. If we can prove that $g$ is the identity map, then we will also know that $f$ is the identity map.\retTwo

		Given any $z_0 \in \Delta$, let $\varphi_{z_0}(z)$ denote the biholomorphism of the form $\frac{z - z_0}{1 - \overline{z_0} z}$. Then note that $h \coloneqq \varphi_{\alpha} \circ g \circ \varphi_{-\alpha}$ is a holomorphism of $\Delta$ with the distinct fixed points $0$ and $\varphi_\alpha(\beta)$. By Schwarz's lemma, we can thus conclude that $h$ is a rotation. Yet the only rotation with a fixed point besides $0$ is the identity map. So, $h$ is the identity map. In turn, so is $g$.
	\end{myIndent}
	
	\item[(b)] Show that the above statement is false for $G = \mathbb{C}$.
	\begin{myIndent}\HexOne
		Let $f(z) = z^2$. Then $f$ is a holomorphic map from $\mathbb{C}$ to $\mathbb{C}$ with $0$ and $1$ as fixed points. This contradicts part (a).
	\end{myIndent}
	
	\item[(c)] Now assume $f: G \to G$ is holomorphic and admits a fixed point $a$ such that $f^\prime(a) = 1$.\\ Show that $f$ must be the identity.
	\begin{myIndent}\HexOne
		Let $\theta: G \to \Delta$ be a biholomorphic function sending $a$ to $0$. Then note that \\$g = \theta \circ f \circ \theta^{-1}$ is a holomorphic function from $\Delta$ to $\Delta$ such that $g(0) = 0$.\\ Also note that:
		
		{\centering\begin{tabular}{l}
			$g^\prime(0) = \theta^\prime(f(\theta^{-1}(0))) \cdot f^\prime(\theta^{-1}(0)) \cdot (\theta^{-1})^\prime(0)$\\ [6pt]
			$\phantom{g^\prime(0)} = \theta^\prime(a) \cdot f^\prime(a) \cdot (\theta^{-1})^\prime(0) = \theta^\prime(a) \cdot f^\prime(a) \cdot \frac{1}{\theta^\prime(\theta^{-1}(0))} = \frac{\theta^\prime(a)}{\theta^\prime(a)} \cdot f^\prime(a) = 1$
		\end{tabular}\retTwo\par}

		It follows by Schwarz' lemma that $g$ is a rotation. Also, since the only rotation with $g^\prime(0) = 1$ is the identity map, we know that $g$ is the identity map. This in turn proves that $f$ is also the identity map. $\blacksquare$
	\end{myIndent}
\end{enumerate}

	In a similar vein, note that if $F: \mcateg{D} \to \mcateg{E}$ and $G, H: \mcateg{C} \to \mcateg{D}$ are functors with\\ $\eta: G \to H$ being a natural transformation / isomorphism, then we can define a\\ natural transformation / isomorphism $\theta: F \circ G \to F \circ H$ by setting $\theta_a = F(\eta_a)$\\ for all $a \in \Obj(\mcateg{C})$.
	\begin{myIndent}\pracTwo
		A key fact I'm implicitely using here is that if $f: a \to b$ is an isomorphism then\\ $F(f): F(a) \to F(b)$ is also an isomorphism for any functor $F$ with an inverse\newpage given by $F(f^{-1})$. This is a result of the fact that functors preserve compositions of morphisms.\retTwo
	\end{myIndent}

	\item As I start to talk more about multi-argument functors (i.e. functors from products of categories), it will be more and more helpful to talk about contravariant functors $F: \mcateg{C} \to \mcateg{D}$ as instead being ordinary functors from $\mcateg{C}^\mathrm{op}$ to $\mcateg{D}$. However, there is a downside to this approach that I want to take note of.
	\begin{myIndent}\hThree
		Suppose $F: \mcateg{C} \to \mcateg{D}$ and $G: \mcateg{D} \to \mcateg{E}$ are contravariant functors. Then the codomain of $F$ and the domain of $G$ are the same and hence $G \circ F$ gives a well-defined mapping of all morphisms and objects (which we can easily check to be a functor).\retTwo

		That said, if we instead view $F$ and $G$ as ordinary functors from opposite categories, then the codomain of $F$ (which is $\mcateg{D}$) and the domain of $G$ (which is $\mcateg{D}^\mathrm{op}$) are no longer the same categories.\retTwo
	\end{myIndent}

	Fortunately, note that for any functor $F: \mcateg{C} \to \mcateg{D}$ we can define a functor\\ $\overline{F}: \mcateg{C}^\mathrm{op} \to \mcateg{D}^\mathrm{op}$ given by $\overline{F}(a) = F(a)$ and $\overline{F}(f^\mathrm{op}) = (F(f))^{\mathrm{op}}$. Then by an abuse of notation, if $G: \mcateg{D}^\mathrm{op} \to \mcateg{E}$ is another functor, we will write $G \circ F$ to denote $G \circ \overline{F}$.

\blab{Set 7 Problem 5:} Suppose $A$ is a unital commutative ring and $K$ is a field.
\begin{enumerate}
	\item[(a)] Suppose $V$ and $W$ are two $K$-vector spaces. Prove that $\dim_K(V \otimes_K W) = \dim_K(V) \cdot \dim_K(W)$
	
	\begin{myIndent}\HexOne
		Firstly, as every $K$-vector space has a $K$-basis we can find isomorphisms\\ $\theta: V \to \bigoplus_{i \in I} K$ and $\psi: W \to \bigoplus_{j \in J}K$. In turn, $\theta \otimes \psi$ is an isomorphism\\ from $V \otimes_K W$ to $(\bigoplus_{i \in I} K) \otimes_K (\bigoplus_{j \in J} K)$.
		
		\begin{myIndent}
			\HexTwoP We know it is an isomorphism since the homomorphism $\theta^{-1} \otimes \psi^{-1}$ acts as\\ an inverse function on all pure tensor elements.\retTwo
		\end{myIndent}

		By set 6 problem 8 (see \inLinkRap{Math 200b Set 6 Problem 8bcd}{pages 691-692}), we thus know that:

		{\centering\begin{tabular}{l}
			$V \otimes_K W \cong (\bigoplus_{i \in I} K) \otimes_K (\bigoplus_{j \in J} K)$\\ [4pt]
			$\phantom{V \otimes_K W} \cong \bigoplus_{j \in J} ((\bigoplus_{i \in I} K) \otimes_K K)$\\ [4pt] 
			$\phantom{V \otimes_K W} \cong \bigoplus_{j \in J} (K \otimes_K (\bigoplus_{i \in I} K))\cong \bigoplus_{j \in J}(\bigoplus_{i \in I} (K \otimes_K K))$ 
		\end{tabular}\retTwo\par}

		Finally, $K \otimes_K K \cong K$ via the isomorphism sending $x \otimes y \to xy$ (see \inLinkRap{Math 200b Proposition page 699}{page 699}). So, we get that:

		{\centering $V \otimes_K W \cong \hspace{-0.5em}\bigoplus\limits_{(i,j) \in I \times J}\hspace{-0.5em} K \Longrightarrow \dim_K(V \otimes_K W) = |I| \cdot |J| = \dim_K(V) \cdot \dim_K(W)$ \retTwo\par}
	\end{myIndent}

	\item[(b)] Suppose $A$ is a local ring. Suppose $M$ and $N$ are finitely generated $A$-modules, and\\ $M \otimes_A N = \{0\}$. Prove that either $M = 0$ or $N = 0$.
	
	\begin{myIndent}\HexOne
		Suppose $\Max(A) = \{\mathfrak{m}\}$ and let $k \coloneqq A/\mathfrak{m}$. Note that $k$ is a field and an $A$-algebra. Hence, we can enrich the tensor functor $T_k$ to be mapping from the category $A$-$\mcateg{mod}$ to the category $k$-$\mcateg{vect}$.\newpage

		Next, by the proposition on \inLinkRap{Math 200b Proposition page 699}{page 699} we know that $k \otimes_A M = \frac{A}{\mathfrak{m}} \otimes_A M \cong \frac{M}{\mathfrak{m}M}$ as\\ $A$-modules via the isomorphism sending $(a + \mathfrak{m}) \otimes x$ to $ax + \mathfrak{m}M$. Yet also recall from set 5 problem 3(b) (see \inLinkRap{Math 200b Set 5 problem 3b}{page 658}) that $\frac{M}{\mathfrak{m}M}$ is a well-defined $k$-vector space when we define $(a + \mathfrak{m})(x + \mathfrak{m}M) = ax + \mathfrak{m}M$. In particular, this means that
		
		{\centering\fontsize{11}{13}\selectfont $(b + \mathfrak{m}) \cdot ((a + \mathfrak{m}) \otimes x) = (ba + \mathfrak{m}) \otimes x \mapsto bax + \mathfrak{m}M = (b+ \mathfrak{m}) \cdot (ax + \mathfrak{m}M)$\retTwo\par}

		And this proves that $k \otimes_A M \cong \frac{M}{\mathfrak{m}M}$ as $k$-vector spaces.\retTwo

		By analogous reasoning and isomorphisms, we have that $k \otimes_A N \cong \frac{N}{\mathfrak{m}N}$ as $k$-vector spaces.\retTwo

		By part (a) plus the fact that $M$ and $N$ are finitely generated (so that we can apply set 5 problem 3(b)), we know that:

		{\centering\fontsize{11}{13}\selectfont\begin{tabular}{l}
			$d(M)d(N) = \dim_k(\frac{M}{\mathfrak{m}M}) \cdot \dim_k(\frac{N}{\mathfrak{m}N}) = \dim_k(\frac{M}{\mathfrak{m}M} \otimes_k \frac{N}{\mathfrak{m}N})$
		\end{tabular}\retTwo\par}

		Yet now define a map $f: M \times N \to \frac{M}{\mathfrak{m}M} \otimes_k \frac{N}{\mathfrak{m}N}$ given by:
		
		{\centering$f(x, y) = (x + \mathfrak{m}M) \otimes (y + \mathfrak{m}N)$.\retTwo\par}

		\begin{myIndent}\HexTwoP
			Here we view $\frac{M}{\mathfrak{m}M} \otimes_k \frac{N}{\mathfrak{m}N}$ as an $A$-module. As a reminder, if $M^\prime$ is an $\frac{A}{\mathfrak{m}}$-module then we can view $M^\prime$ as an $A$-module by setting $a \cdot x = (a + \mathfrak{m})x$ for all $a \in A$ and $x \in M^\prime$.\retTwo
		\end{myIndent}

		This is easily checked to be $A$-bilinear. So, by the universal property of tensor products there exists a unique $A$-module homomorphism $\widehat{f}: M \otimes_A N \to \frac{M}{\mathfrak{m}M} \otimes_k \frac{N}{\mathfrak{m}N}$ satisfying that $\widehat{f}(x \otimes y) = (x + \mathfrak{m}M) \otimes (y + \mathfrak{m}N)$ for all $x \in M$ and $y \in N$. Yet as $M \otimes_A N = \{0\}$ and the image of $\widehat{f}$ is easily seen to include all pure tensors, this implies that all pure tensors of $\frac{M}{\mathfrak{m}M} \otimes_k \frac{N}{\mathfrak{m}N}$ are the zero element. And as they span the entire vector space, we have that $\dim_k(\frac{M}{\mathfrak{m}M} \otimes_k \frac{N}{\mathfrak{m}N}) = 0$. This proves that either $d(M) = 0$ or $d(N) = 0$, which in turn implies that either $M = \{0\}$ or $N = \{0\}$.\retTwo
	\end{myIndent}

	\item[(c)] Recall that $\supp(M) \coloneqq \{\mathfrak{p} \in \Spec(A) : M_\mathfrak{p} \neq \{0\}\}$ (see \inLinkRap{Math 200b Set 6 Problem 2}{page 672}). Suppose that $M$ and $N$ are finitely generated $A$-modules. Prove that $\supp(M \otimes_A N) = \supp(M) \cap \supp(N)$.
	\begin{myIndent}\HexOne
		By problem 4 of this problem set, we know that $(M \otimes_A N)_\mathfrak{p} \cong M_\mathfrak{p} \otimes_{A_\mathfrak{p}} N_\mathfrak{p}$. Also as $A_\mathfrak{p}$ is a local ring and $M_\mathfrak{p}$ and $N_\mathfrak{p}$ are finitely generated (since $M$ and $N$ are), we know from part (b) of this problem that $M_\mathfrak{p} \otimes_{A_\mathfrak{p}} N_\mathfrak{p} = \{0\}$ iff $M_\mathfrak{p} = \{0\}$ or $N_\mathfrak{p} = \{0\}$.\retTwo

		Therefore $(M \otimes_A N)_\mathfrak{p} = \{0\}$ iff $\mathfrak{p} \notin \supp(M)$ or $\mathfrak{p} \notin \supp(N)$. And this proves that $\supp(M \otimes_A N) = \supp(M) \cap \supp(N)$. $\blacksquare$\retTwo
	\end{myIndent}
\end{enumerate}
