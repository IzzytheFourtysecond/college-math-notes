\Hstatement\blab{Set 6 Problem 1:} Let $a, b \in \Delta - \{0\}$ and consider the twice punctured disks $D_1 = \Delta - \{0, a\}$ and $D_2 = \Delta - \{0, b\}$. Find a necessary and sufficient condition for $D_1$ and $D_2$ to be biholomorphic and determine all biholomorphic maps $f: D_1 \to D_2$.
\begin{myIndent}\HexOne
	Firstly, we find our necessary condition. Suppose $f: D_1 \to D_2$ is a biholomorphic function.\\ [1pt] Then as $|f(z)| \leq 1$ for all $z \in D_1$, we know that $f$ has a removable singularity at $0$ and\\ [-1pt] $a$. Hence, we may define a holomorphic extension $\widetilde{f}$ of $f$ to the entire unit disk. Also note that $\widetilde{f}(\Delta) \subseteq \overline{\Delta}$ and is an open set by the open map theorem. It follows that $\widetilde{f}$ is a\\ [2pt] holomorphic function from $\Delta$ to $\Delta$.\retTwo

	Suppose for the sake of contradiction that there exists $c \in \Delta - \{0\}$ such that\\ $\widetilde{f}(0) = \widetilde{f}(c) = \alpha$.
	\begin{myIndent}\HexTwoP
		Then pick disjoint disks $\Delta_0$ and $\Delta_c$ centered at $0$ and $c$. By the open mapping theorem, we can find a disk $\Delta_\alpha$ centered at $\alpha$ such that $\Delta_\alpha \subseteq \widetilde{f}(\Delta_0)$ and $\Delta_\alpha \subseteq \widetilde{f}(\Delta_c)$. Pick $\beta \in \Delta_\alpha - \{\alpha, \widetilde{f}(a)\}$. Then there exists $u \in \Delta_0 - \{0\}$ and $v \in \Delta_c - \{c\}$ such that $\widetilde{f}(u) = \beta = \widetilde{f}(v)$. Importantly, neither $u$ nor $v$ equals $0$ or $a$. So, $u$ and $v$ are in $D_1$.\\ [2pt] And this contradicts that $f$ is injective on $D_1$.\retTwo
	\end{myIndent}

	By analogous reasoning, we can conclude there doesn't exist $c \in \Delta - \{a\}$ such that\\ $\widetilde{f}(a) = \widetilde{f}(c)$. Consequently, we can conclude that:

	{\centering$\widetilde{f} \in \Aut(\Delta)$ and $\{\widetilde{f}(0), \widetilde{f}(a)\} = \{0, b\}$\retTwo\par}

	Now any automorphism of $\Delta$ has the form $e^{i\theta}\frac{z - z_0}{1 - \overline{z_0}z}$ where $z_0$ the element of $\Delta$ that is\\ mapped to $0$ and $\theta \in \mathbb{R}$. Hence, we have two cases:
	\begin{itemize}
		\item If $\widetilde{f}(0) = 0$ then we know $\widetilde{f}(z) = e^{i\theta}z$ for some $\theta \in \mathbb{R}$. Also $b = \widetilde{f}(a) = e^{i\theta}a$ implies that $\frac{b}{a} = e^{i\theta}$. This shows that $|a| = |b|$ and that $\widetilde{f}(z) = \frac{b}{a}z$.\retTwo
		
		\item If $\widetilde{f}(a) = 0$ then we know $\widetilde{f}(z) = e^{i\theta}\frac{z - a}{1 - \overline{a}z}$ for some $\theta \in \mathbb{R}$. Also, $b = \widetilde{f}(0) = e^{i\theta}\frac{-a}{1}$ implies that $\frac{-b}{a} = e^{i\theta}$. This shows that $|a| = |b|$ and that $\widetilde{f}(z) = \frac{-b}{a} \cdot \frac{z-a}{1-\overline{a}z}$.\retTwo
	\end{itemize}

	This proves that if $D_1$ and $D_2$ are biholomorphic then we have that $|a| = |b|$ and the only two biholomorphisms are $z \mapsto \frac{b}{a}z$ and $z \mapsto \frac{-b}{a} \cdot \frac{z -a}{1 -\overline{a}z}$.\retTwo

	To finish off, this is also a sufficient condition. If $|a| = |b|$ then both of the above mappings are biholomorphisms of the unit disk satisfying that $\{0, a\}$ is mapped to $\{0, b\}$. $\blacksquare$\retTwo
\end{myIndent}

\hTwo We can also characterize the biholomorphisms of the upper half plane (which I'll denote as $\mathfrak{h}^+$).
\begin{myIndent}\pracTwo
	Note that any biholomorphism of $\mathfrak{h}^+$ can be written as $c^{-1} \circ f \circ c$ where $c: \mathfrak{h}^+ \to \Delta$ is the Cayley transform and $f$ is a biholomorphism of $\Delta$.\retTwo

	Consider any matrix $\left[\begin{smallmatrix} A & B \\ \overline{B} & \overline{A}\end{smallmatrix}\right] \in \mathrm{SU}(1,1)$ associated with $f$ and let $\left[\begin{smallmatrix} 1 & -i \\ 1 & i\end{smallmatrix}\right]$ and $\frac{1}{2}\left[\begin{smallmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ i & -i\end{smallmatrix}\right]$ be the matrices associated with $c$ and $c^{-1}$ respectively. Then the matrix associated with $c^{-1} \circ f \circ c$ is $\left[\begin{smallmatrix} \rea{A} + \rea{B}  & \ima{A} - \ima{B} \\ -\ima{A} - \ima{B} & \rea{A} - \rea{B}\end{smallmatrix}\right] \in \GL_2(\mathbb{R})$. Also, we scaled the matrices associated with $c$ and $c^{-1}$ so that they'd have determinants $2i$ and $\frac{-i}{2}$ respectively. Therefore, our real matrix has determinant 1.\newpage

	Combining this with an algebra homework exercise (see \inLinkRap{Math 200a Homework Set 2 Problem 3}{page 286}), we can conclude that the set of biholomorphisms of $\mathfrak{h}^+$ is isomorphic to $\mathrm{PSL}_2(\mathbb{R})$.\retTwo
\end{myIndent}

One other note to quickly make is that there is no biholomorphism from $\mathbb{C}$ to $\Delta$ (We know this by Liouville's theorem).\retTwo

\Hstatement\blab{Set 6 Problem 2:} The pseudo-hyperbolic distance on $\Delta$ is given by $d(z, w) = \left|\frac{z - w}{1 - \overline{z} w}\right|$.
\begin{enumerate}
	\item[(a)] Show by direct calculation that if $f$ is a biholomorphism of $\Delta$ then
	
	{\centering$d(z, w) = d(f(z), f(w))$.\retTwo\par}

	\begin{myIndent}\HexOne
		Recall that any biholomorphism of $f$ is a composition of a rotation $z \mapsto e^{i\theta}z$\\ (where $\theta \in \mathbb{R})$ and another function of the form $\varphi_{a}(z) = \frac{z - a}{1 - \overline{a}z}$ where $a \in \Delta$.\\ Therefore, it suffices to show that rotations and functions of the form $\varphi_a$\\ preserve pseudo-hyperbolic distance.

		\begin{itemize}
			\item $d(e^{i\theta}z, e^{i\theta}w) = \left|\frac{e^{i\theta}z - e^{i\theta}w}{1 - e^{-i\theta}\overline{z}e^{i\theta}w}\right| = |e^{i\theta}| \cdot \left|\frac{z - w}{1 - 1\overline{z}w}\right| = 1 \cdot d(z, w)$\retTwo
			
			\item $d(\varphi_a(z), \varphi_a(w)) = \left|\frac{\frac{z - a}{1 - \overline{a}z} - \frac{w - a}{1 - \overline{a}w}}{1 - \left(\frac{\overline{z} - \overline{a}}{1 - a\overline{z}}\right)\left( \frac{w - a}{1 - \overline{a}w}\right)}\right|$\\ [6pt]
			$\phantom{d(\varphi_a(z), \varphi_a(w))} = \left|\frac{\frac{(1 - \overline{a}w)(z- a) - (1 - \overline{a}z)(w - a)}{(1 - \overline{a}z)(1 - \overline{a}w)}}{\frac{(1 - a\overline{z})(1 - \overline{a}w) - (\overline{z} - \overline{a})(w - a)}{(1 - a\overline{z})(1 - \overline{a}w)}}\right|$\\ [6pt]
			$\phantom{d(\varphi_a(z), \varphi_a(w))} = \left|\frac{1 - a\overline{z}}{1 - \overline{a}z} \right| \cdot \left| \frac{1 - \overline{a}w}{1 - \overline{a}w}\right| \cdot \left|\frac{(1 - \overline{a}w)(z- a) - (1 - \overline{a}z)(w - a)}{(1 - a\overline{z})(1 - \overline{a}w) - (\overline{z} - \overline{a})(w - a)}\right|$\\ [6pt]
			$\phantom{d(\varphi_a(z), \varphi_a(w))} = 1 \cdot 1 \cdot \left|\frac{z - a - \overline{a} wz + |a|^2w - w + a + \overline{a}wz - |a|^2 z}{1 - \overline{a}w - a\overline{z} + |a|^2 w \overline{z} - w\overline{z} + a\overline{z} + \overline{a}w - |a|^2}\right|$\\ [6pt]
			$\phantom{d(\varphi_a(z), \varphi_a(w))} = \left|\frac{z + |a|^2w - w - |a|^2 z}{1  + |a|^2 w \overline{z} - w\overline{z} - |a|^2}\right| = \left|\frac{(z - w)(1 - |a|^2)}{(1 - \overline{z}w)(1 - |a|^2)}\right| = \left|\frac{z - w}{1 - \overline{z}w}\right| = d(z, w)$\retTwo
		\end{itemize}
	\end{myIndent}

	\item[(b)] Let $f: \Delta \to \Delta$ be holomorphic. Using (a) to reduce to a familiar case, show that\\ $d(f(z), f(w)) \leq d(z, w)$. Thus holomorphic maps contract pseudo-hyperbolic distance.
	
	\begin{myIndent}\HexOne
		Consider the biholomorphisms $\varphi_{-w}$ and $\varphi_{f(w)}$ of the unit disk. Then upon setting\\ $\alpha = \varphi_{w}(z)$, we have by part (a) that:

		{\centering\begin{tabular}{l}
			$d(f(z), f(w)) = d((\varphi_{f(w)} \circ f )(z), (\varphi_{f(w)} \circ f)(w)) = d((\varphi_{f(w)} \circ f )(z), 0)$\\ [6pt]
			$\phantom{d(f(z), f(w)) = d((\varphi_{f(w)} \circ f )(z), (\varphi_{f(w)} \circ f)(w))} = d((\varphi_{f(w)} \circ f \circ \varphi_{-w})(\alpha), 0)$\\ [6pt]
			$\phantom{d(f(z), f(w)) = d((\varphi_{f(w)} \circ f )(z), (\varphi_{f(w)} \circ f)(w))} = \left|\frac{(\varphi_{f(w)} \circ f \circ \varphi_{-w})(\alpha) - 0}{1 - 0}\right|$\\ [6pt]
		\end{tabular}\retTwo\par}

		Yet now as $\varphi_{f(w)} \circ f \circ \varphi_{-w}$ is a holomorphism from $\Delta$ to $\Delta$ sending $0$ to $0$, we know by Schwarz' lemma that $|(\varphi_{f(w)} \circ f \circ \varphi_{-w})(\alpha)| \leq |\alpha|$. Hence, we may conclude that $d(f(z), f(w)) \leq |\alpha| = |\varphi_w(z)| = \frac{|z - w|}{|1 - \overline{w}z|} = \frac{|z - w|}{|1 - \overline{z}w|} = d(z, w)$.\newpage
	\end{myIndent}

	\item[(c)] Suppose $f: \Delta \to \Delta$ is a holomorphic function and that there exists distinct $z, w \in \Delta$\\ with $d(f(z), f(w)) = d(z, w)$. Show that $f$ is a biholomorphism and consequently\\ by (a) that $d(f(z), f(w)) = d(z, w)$ for all $z, w \in \Delta$.
	\begin{myIndent}\HexOne
		Like in part (b), write:
		
		{\centering$d(f(z), f(w)) = |(\varphi_{f(w)} \circ f \circ \varphi_{-w})(\varphi_w(z))| \leq |\varphi_w(z)| = d(z, w)$.\retTwo\par}
		
		This time however, note that because $|(\varphi_{f(w)} \circ f \circ \varphi_{-w})(\varphi_w(z))| = |\varphi_w(z)|$ (since $d(f(z), f(w)) = d(z, w)$) and $\varphi_w(z) \neq 0$ (since $z \neq w$), we must have by Schwarz'\\ lemma that $\varphi_{f(w)} \circ f \circ \varphi_{-w}$ is a rotation $R$. It follows that $f = \varphi_{-f(w)} \circ R \circ \varphi_w$ is a biholomorphism.\retTwo 
	\end{myIndent}

	\item[(d)] Show that $d$ is a metric.
	\begin{myIndent}\HexOne
		Firstly, it's obvious that $d(z, w) = 0$ iff $z = w$ and that $d(z, w) = d(w, z)$ (just take the complex conjugate of the denominator\dots). Thus, the only hard part is to show that $d$ satisfies the triangle inequality. In other words, we want to show that if $z, w, s \in \Delta$ then $d(z, s) \leq d(z, w) + d(w, s)$.

		\begin{myIndent}\HexTwoP
			To start off, by applying $\varphi_s$ to $z, w, s$ and invoking part (a), we can assume\\ without loss of generality that $s = 0$. Hence, it suffices to prove that\\ $d(z, 0) \leq d(z, w) + d(w, 0)$ for all $z, w \in \Delta$.\retTwo

			Next, by applying a rotation in $\Delta$ to $z, w, 0$ and then invoking part (a), we can assume without loss of generality that $z$ is a nonnegative real number. Hence, we may now write $z = r_1$ and $w = r_2e^{i\theta}$ where $r_1, r_2 \in [0, 1)$ and $\theta \in \mathbb{R}$. In\\ turn:
			\begin{itemize}
				\item $d(z, 0) = r_1$.
				\item $d(z, w) + d(w, 0) = \left|\frac{r_1 - r_2 e^{i\theta}}{1 - r_1r_2 e^{i\theta}}\right| + r_2$.\retTwo
			\end{itemize}

			Note that if $r_2 = 0$ then $|d(z, w)| = |\frac{r_1 - 0}{1 - 0}| = r_1$ and so our inequality\\ trivially holds. So, we may assume $r_2 \neq 0$. Also note that if $r_2 \geq r_1$, then we are already done. So, we may assume now that $r_2 < r_1$ (which happens to give us that $r_2, r_1 \neq 0$). In turn we know that $r_1 - r_2 > 0$. So, our desired inequality is equivalent to saying that:\\ [-20pt]

			{\centering\begin{tabular}{l}
				$(r_1 - r_2)^2 \leq \left|\frac{r_1 - r_2 e^{i\theta}}{1 - r_1r_2 e^{i\theta}}\right|^2 = \frac{\left|r_1 - r_2 e^{i\theta}\right|^2}{\left|1 - r_1r_2 e^{i\theta}\right|^2} = \frac{(r_1 - r_2 e^{i\theta})(r_1 - r_2 e^{-i\theta})}{(1 - r_1r_2 e^{i\theta})(1 - r_1r_2 e^{-i\theta})}$\\ [6pt]
				$\phantom{(r_2 - r_1)^2 \leq \left|\frac{r_1 - r_2 e^{i\theta}}{1 - r_1r_2 e^{i\theta}}\right|^2 = \frac{\left|r_1 - r_2 e^{i\theta}\right|^2}{\left|1 - r_1r_2 e^{i\theta}\right|^2}} = \frac{r_1^2 - 2r_1r_2 \rea{e^{i\theta}} + r_2^2}{1 - 2r_1r_2 \rea{e^{i\theta}} + r_1^2r_2^2}$ 
			\end{tabular}\retTwo\par}

			Therefore, it suffices to show that $(r_1 - r_2)^2 \leq \frac{r_1^2 - 2r_1r_2 \cos(\theta) + r_2^2}{1 - 2r_1r_2\cos(\theta) + r_1^2r_2^2}$.\retTwo

			Or in other words:

			{\centering $(r_1^2 - 2r_1r_2 + r_2^2)(1 - 2r_1r_2 \cos(\theta) + r_1^2r_2^2) \leq r_1^2 - 2r_1r_2 \cos(\theta) + r_2^2$ \retTwo\par}

			Upon expanding and canceling, we get that:

			{\centering $(-2r_1r_2 + r_1^4 r_2^2  - 2r_1^3 r_2^3 + r_1^2 r_2^4) - 2r_1r_2(r_1 - r_2)^2 \cos(\theta) \leq -2r_1r_2\cos(\theta)$\retTwo\par}

			Then by dividing by $r_1r_2$ and rearranging a little more:\newpage

			{\centering $(-2 + r_1^3 r_2  - 2r_1^2 r_2^2 + r_1 r_2^3) \leq 2((r_1 - r_2)^2 - 1) \cdot \cos(\theta)$\retTwo\par}

			Finally, the right side is minimized when $\cos(\theta) = +1$. So, we need to show that $(-2 + r_1^3 r_2  - 2r_1^2 r_2^2 + r_1 r_2^3) \leq 2((r_1 - r_2)^2 - 1)$. Fortunately, this is equivalent to saying that:

			{\centering\begin{tabular}{l}
				$0 \leq 2(r_1 - r_2)^2 - (r_1^3r_2 - 2r_1^2r_2^2 + r_1r_2^3)$\\ [4pt]
				$\phantom{0} = 2(r_1 - r_2)^2 - r_1r_2(r_1^2 - 2r_1r_2 + r_2^2) = 2(r_1 - r_2)^2 - r_1r_2(r_1 - r_2)^2$\\ [4pt]
				$\phantom{0 = 2(r_1 - r_2)^2 - r_1r_2(r_1^2 - 2r_1r_2 + r_2^2)} = (2 -r_1r_2)(r_1 - r_2)^2$\\ [4pt]
			\end{tabular}\retTwo\par}

			Then as $r_1, r_2 \in (0, 1)$ and $r_2 < r_1$, we know that $(2 - r_1r_2) > 0$ and\\ $(r_1 - r_2) > 0$. So this last inequality holds and we are done.\retTwo
		\end{myIndent}
	\end{myIndent}

	\item[(e)] As an application to (b), assume $f: \Delta \to \Delta$ is a holomorphic function satisfying that $f(\frac{1}{2}) = \frac{1}{4}$. Show that $\frac{1}{21} \leq |f(\frac{1}{3})| \leq \frac{9}{19}$.
	
	\begin{myIndent}\HexOne
		Firstly, note that:

		{\centering$\left|\frac{\frac{1}{4} - f(\frac{1}{3})}{1 - \frac{1}{4}f(\frac{1}{3})}\right| = d(\frac{1}{4}, f(\frac{1}{3})) \leq d(\frac{1}{2}, \frac{1}{3}) = \left|\frac{\frac{1}{2} - \frac{1}{3}}{1 - \frac{1}{6}}\right| = \frac{\sfrac{1}{6}}{\sfrac{5}{6}} = \frac{1}{5}$\retTwo\par}

		It follows that $5|\frac{1}{4} - f(\frac{1}{3})| \leq |1 - \frac{1}{4}f(\frac{1}{3})|$. By triangle inquality and reverse triangle inquality, we get that:

		{\centering $5\left| \frac{1}{4} - |f(\frac{1}{3})|\right| \leq 1 + \frac{1}{4}|f(\frac{1}{3})|$ \retTwo\par}

		Case 1: Suppose $|f(\frac{1}{3})| \leq \frac{1}{4}$.
		\begin{myIndent}\HexTwoP
			Then $\frac{5}{4} - 5|f(\frac{1}{3})| \leq 1 + \frac{1}{4}|f(\frac{1}{3})| \Longrightarrow \frac{1}{4} \leq \frac{21}{4}|f(\frac{1}{3})| \Longrightarrow \frac{1}{21} \leq |f(\frac{1}{3})|$\retTwo
		\end{myIndent}

		Case 2: Suppose $|f(\frac{1}{3})| \geq \frac{1}{4}$.
		\begin{myIndent}\HexTwoP
			Then $5|f(\frac{1}{3})| - \frac{5}{4} \leq 1 + \frac{1}{4}|f(\frac{1}{3})| \Longrightarrow \frac{19}{4}|f(\frac{1}{3})| \leq \frac{9}{4} \Longrightarrow |f(\frac{1}{3})| \leq \frac{9}{19}$.\retTwo
		\end{myIndent}
	\end{myIndent}

	\item[(f)] Let $f: \Delta \to \Delta$ be holomorphic and satisfy that $f(\frac{1}{3}) = \frac{1}{2}$. Show that $f$ has no zeros in the disc $\{z : |z| < \frac{1}{7}\}$.
	\begin{myIndent}\HexTwoP
		Note that if $f(w) = 0$ for some $w \in \Delta$, then by part (b) we must have that:

		{\centering $\frac{1}{2} = d(\frac{1}{2}, 0) \leq d(\frac{1}{3}, w) = \left|\frac{\frac{1}{3} - w}{1 - \frac{w}{3}}\right| = \left|\frac{1 - 3w}{3 - w}\right| \Longrightarrow |3 - w| \leq 2|1 - 3w|$ \retTwo\par}

		Therefore, to show that $f$ has no zeros in the disk $\{z : |z| < \frac{1}{7}\}$, it suffices to show that there is some $\alpha > 0$ with $2|1 - 3w| < \alpha < |3 - w|$ for all $w$ in the proposed disk. Fortunately, if $|w| < \frac{1}{7}$ then $2|1 - 3w| < 2(1 + \frac{3}{7}) = \frac{20}{7}$ and $|3 - w| > 3 - \frac{1}{7} = \frac{20}{7}$. So, $\alpha = \frac{20}{7}$ works. $\blacksquare$\retTwo
	\end{myIndent}
\end{enumerate}

\blab{Set 6 Problem 3:} Let $f_n : \Delta \to \Delta$ be a family of biholomorphisms of $\Delta$ converging locally uniformly to a nonconstant function $f$. Show that $f$ is an automorphism of $\Delta$.
\begin{myIndent}\HexOne
	Firstly, note that because $f$ is nonconstant (by assumption) and holomorphic (by the\\ Weierstaß convergence), we know that $f(\Delta)$ is an open set in $\mathbb{C}$. Also, as $f_n(\Delta) \subseteq \Delta$ for\\ [-1pt] all $n$, we can conclude that $f(\Delta) \subseteq (\overline{\Delta})^\circ = \Delta$. Thus $f$ maps $\Delta$ to $\Delta$. And with that we've shown that $d(f(a), f(b))$ is well-defined for all $a, b \in \Delta$.\newpage

	Next note that if $d$ is the distance function defined in the prior problem, then $d$ is a\\ continuous function from $\Delta \times \Delta$ to $\Delta$. Thus for any $a, b\in \Delta$, because we have that\\ $f(a), f(b) \in \Delta$, that $f_n(a) \to f(a)$, and that $f_n(b) \to f(b)$, we can conclude that\\ $d(f_n(a), f_n(b)) \to d(f(a), f(b))$ as $n \to \infty$. Simultaneously though, we know by part (a) of the last problem that $d(f_n(a), f_n(b)) = d(a, b)$ for all $n \in \mathbb{N}$. Hence, this proves that $d(f(a), f(b)) = d(a, b)$ for all $a, b \in \Delta$. By part (c) of the last problem, we can thus conclude that $f$ is a biholomorphism.\retTwo

	\begin{myIndent}\color{red}
		As a side note, the first paragraph is the only part of this proof where we\\ specifically need $f$ to be nonconstant. If $f$ were non-constant, then you could imagine that for any $a, b \in \Delta$ we'd have that $d(f_n(a), f_n(b)) = d(a, b)$ for all $n$ but that also $f_n(a), f_n(b)$ race towards the boundary of $\Delta$. 
		\begin{myIndent}\color{orange}
			For an example of this problematic behavior, consider the functions\\ $f_n(z) = \frac{z - (1 - \frac{1}{n})}{1 - (1 - \frac{1}{n})z}$. Each $f_n$ is a biholomorphism of $\Delta$. Also, we can easily\\ see that $f_n(z)$ converges locally uniformly to $-1$.\retTwo

			After all, suppose $|z| \leq r < 1$. Then:
			
			{\centering$f_n(z) + 1 = \frac{z - (1 - \frac{1}{n}) + 1 - (1 - \frac{1}{n})z}{1 - (1 - \frac{1}{n})z} = \frac{(z + 1)(1 - (1 - \frac{1}{n}))}{1 - (1 - \frac{1}{n})z} = \frac{(z + 1)}{n - (n - 1)z}$\retTwo\par}

			It follows that:
			
			{\centering$|f_n(z) + 1| = \frac{|z + 1|}{|n - (n-1)z|} \leq \frac{|z| + 1}{n - (n-1)|z|} \leq \frac{r+1}{n - (n - 1)r} = \frac{r + 1}{n(1 - r) + r}$\retTwo\par}

			And since $r < 1$, it follows that $\frac{r + 1}{n(1 - r) + r} \to 0$ as $n \to \infty$. This finishes showing that our example $f_n$ converge locally uniformly to $-1$.\retTwo
		\end{myIndent}
	\end{myIndent}
\end{myIndent}

As a fairly easy corollary, if $\widehat{j}_i$ is the embedding of $M_i$ into $\bigoplus_{i \in I}M_i$, then we'd get that $(n_i \otimes m_i)_{i \in I}$ maps to $\sum_{i \in I}n_i \otimes \widehat{j}_i(m_i)$.

\hTwo Now back to the algebra we were learning in class.\retTwo

Recall that for any two $A$-modules $M_1, M_2$, we have a bilinear map $\iota : M_1 \times M_2 \to M_1 \otimes M_2$ given by $(m_1, m_2) \mapsto m_1 \otimes m_2$. From that we can deduce a few arithmetic properties of the tensor product. Namely:\newpage
\begin{itemize}
	\item $(am_1)\otimes m_2 = a(m_1 \otimes m_2) = m_1 \otimes (am_2)$ {\color{red}(this property is called \udefine{$A$ balance}\dots)},
	\item $(m_1 + m^\prime_1) \otimes m_2 = (m_1 \otimes m_2) + (m^\prime_1 \otimes m_2)$,
	\item $m_1 \otimes (m_2 + m_2^\prime) = (m_1 \otimes m_2) + (m_1 \otimes m_2^\prime)$.
\end{itemize}

One more note before I start covering propositions is that if there is ambiguity about\\ what ring I'm taking a tensor product with respect to, I'll add a subscript to the $\otimes$ symbol.\\ I specifically mention this because if $M_1$ and $M_2$ can both be interpretted as either\\ $A$-modules or $B$-modules, then $M_1 \otimes_A M_2$ and $M_1 \otimes_B M_2$ will be different modules.\retTwo

\exTwo\ul{Proposition:} If $M$ is an $A$-module and $\mathfrak{a} \lhd A$, then $\frac{A}{\mathfrak{a}} \otimes M \cong \frac{M}{\mathfrak{a}M}$.
\begin{myIndent}\exThreeP
	Proof:\\
	Let $f: \frac{A}{\mathfrak{a}} \times M \to  \frac{M}{\mathfrak{a}M}$ be defined by $f(x + \mathfrak{a}, m) = xm + \mathfrak{a}M$. To see that $f$ is well-\\defined, suppose $x^\prime = x + a$ for some $a \in \mathfrak{a}$. Then: 
	
	{\centering$x^\prime m  = (x + a)m = xm + am \Longrightarrow x^\prime m + \mathfrak{a}M = xm + \mathfrak{a}M$.\retTwo\par}

	Also note that $f$ is $A$-bilinear.
	\begin{myIndent}\exPPP
		After all, we have that

		{\centering\begin{tabular}{l}
			$f(a_1(x_1 + \mathfrak{a}) + a_2(x_2 + \mathfrak{a}), m) = f((a_1x_1 + a_2x_2) + \mathfrak{a}, m)$\\ [4pt]
			$\phantom{f(a_1(x_1 + \mathfrak{a}) + a_2(x_2 + \mathfrak{a}), m)} = (a_1x_1 + a_2x_2)m + \mathfrak{a}M$\\ [4pt]
			$\phantom{f(a_1(x_1 + \mathfrak{a}) + a_2(x_2 + \mathfrak{a}), m)} = a_1(x_1 m + \mathfrak{a}M) + a_2(x_2 m + \mathfrak{a}M)$\\ [4pt]
			$\phantom{f(a_1(x_1 + \mathfrak{a}) + a_2(x_2 + \mathfrak{a}), m)} = a_1f(x_1, m) + a_2f(x_2, m)$
		\end{tabular}\retTwo\par}

		Meanwhile, linearity in the other argument is proven identically.\retTwo
	\end{myIndent}

	Thus by the universal property of tensor products, there must exist a unique $A$-module\\ homomorphism $\widehat{f}: \frac{A}{\mathfrak{a}} \otimes M \to \frac{M}{\mathfrak{a}M}$ such that $\widehat{f}((x + \mathfrak{a}) \otimes m) = xm + \mathfrak{a}M$. To show\\ that this is an isomorphism, we next construct an inverse homomorphism.\retTwo

	Define $g: M \to \frac{A}{\mathfrak{a}} \otimes M$ by $g(m) = (1 + \mathfrak{a}) \otimes m$. By the properties at the top of the page, we know that $g$ is an $A$-module homomorphism. Also, we claim that $\mathfrak{a}M \subseteq \ker(g)$.
	\begin{myIndent}\exPPP
		After all, note that every element of $\mathfrak{a}M$ is of the form $\sum_{i=1}^k a_i m_i$ for some $a_i \in \mathfrak{a}$ and $m_i \in M$. So to show that $\mathfrak{a}M \subseteq \ker(g)$ it suffices to prove that $g(am) = 0$ for all $a \in \mathfrak{a}$ and $m \in M$. But this is easy.

		{\centering $g(am) = (1 + \mathfrak{a}) \otimes am = (a + \mathfrak{a}) \otimes m = (0 + \mathfrak{a}) \otimes m = 0$ \retTwo\par}
	\end{myIndent}

	It follows that there is a well-defined $A$-module homomorphism $\overline{g} : \frac{M}{\mathfrak{a}M} \to \frac{A}{\mathfrak{a}} \otimes M$ given by $\overline{g}(m + \mathfrak{a}M) = (1 + \mathfrak{a}) \otimes m$.\retTwo

	Claim: $\widehat{f} \circ \overline{g} = \myId_{\frac{M}{\mathfrak{a}M}}$.
	\begin{myIndent}\exPPP
		$\widehat{f} \circ \overline{g}(m + \mathfrak{a}M) = \widehat{f}((1 + \mathfrak{a}) \otimes m) = m + \mathfrak{a}M$.\retTwo
	\end{myIndent}

	Claim: $\overline{g} \circ \widehat{f} = \myId_{\frac{A}{\mathfrak{a}} \otimes M}$.
	\begin{myIndent}\exPPP
		Since $\frac{A}{\mathfrak{a}} \otimes M$ is generated by pure tensor elements, it is enough to check the claim at a pure tensor element $(x + \mathfrak{a}) \otimes m$.

		{\centering $\overline{g}(\widehat{f}((x + \mathfrak{a}) \otimes m)) = \overline{g}(xm + \mathfrak{a}M) = (1 + \mathfrak{a}) \otimes xm = (x + \mathfrak{a}) \otimes m$. \exThreeP$\blacksquare$\newpage\par}
	\end{myIndent}
\end{myIndent}

	Let $\Obj(\mcateg{D}) = \{(a_1, a_2) : a_1 \in \Obj(\mcateg{C}_1) \text{ and } a_2 \in \Obj(\mcateg{C}_2)\}$ and for all $(a_1, a_2), (b_1, b_2)$\\ in $\Obj(\mcateg{D})$ define $\Hom_\mcateg{D}((a_1, a_2), (b_1, b_2))$ to be the class of all $(f, g)$ where\\ $f \in \Hom_{\mcateg{C}_1}(a_1, b_1)$ and $g \in \Hom_{\mcateg{C}_2}(a_2, b_2)$. Also define morphism composition\\ to be pairwise. Then $\mcateg{D}$ is easily checked to be a small category.\retTwo

	Next, for each $i \in I$ define the functor $\pi_i : \mcateg{D} \to \mcateg{C}_i$ such that $\pi_i((a_1, a_2)) = a_i$ and $\pi_i((f_1, f_2)) \mapsto f_i$. It is easily checked that each $\pi_i$ is a well-defined functor.\retTwo

	Finally, suppose $F_1 : \mcateg{E} \to \mcateg{C}_1$ and $F_2: \mcateg{E} \to \mcateg{C}_2$ are functors. Then we want to show that there is a unique functor $\widetilde{F} : \mcateg{E} \to \mcateg{D}$ such that $F_i = \pi_i \circ \widetilde{F}$ for both $i$. Yet note that $\widetilde{F}(a)$ must equal $(F_1(a), F_2(a))$ for all $a \in \Obj(\mcateg{E})$ in order for $\pi_i \circ \widetilde{F} = F_i$ for both $i$. Similarly, we must have that $(f: a \to b) \mapsto (F_1(f), F_2(f))$ for all morphisms $f: a \to b$ in $\mcateg{E}$.\newpage

	Upon checking that $\widetilde{F}$ defined as we said was required is in fact a functor, this proves that $\mcateg{D}$ is a product of $\mcateg{C}_1$ and $\mcateg{C}_2$. $\blacksquare$\retTwo
\end{myIndent}

\hTwo Importantly, no part of the prior construction fails even if $\mcateg{C}_1$ and $\mcateg{C}_2$ aren't small categories. So, in general, we define $\mcateg{C}_1 \times \mcateg{C}_2$ such that:
\begin{itemize}
	\item its class of objects the class of all pairs of objects $(a_1, a_2)$ where $a_1 \in \Obj(\mcateg{C}_1)$ and $a_2 \in \Obj(\mcateg{C}_2)$;
	\item $\Hom_{\mcateg{C}_1 \times \mcateg{C}_2}((a_1, a_2), (b_1, b_2))$ is the collection of all pairs of morphisms $(f, g)$ where $f: a_1 \to b_1$ is a morphism in $\Hom_{\mcateg{C}_1}(a_1, b_1)$ and $g: a_2 \to b_2$ is a morphism in $\Hom_{\mcateg{C}_2}(a_2, b_2)$;
	\item morphism composition is pairwise.\retTwo
\end{itemize}

\exTwo\ul{The Riemann Mapping Theorem:} If $G \neq \mathbb{C}$ is a root domain, then $U$ is biholomorphic\\ to $\Delta$.
\begin{myIndent}\exThreeP
	Proof:\\
	Pick any $a \in G$.\retTwo
	\begin{myIndent}\myComment
		Our first goal will be to show that there exists an injective holomorphic function\\ $f: G \to \Delta$ such that $f(a) = 0$. That way, we can say that the family of functions $\mathcal{F} \coloneqq \{f : G \to \Delta : f \text{ is injective, holomorphic, and} f(a) = 0\}$ is nonempty.
		\begin{myIndent}
			As a side note, in the original German literature these functions are called\\ \udefine{Schlicht functions}.\retTwo
		\end{myIndent}
	\end{myIndent}

	Pick $b \in G^\comp$ and define $h(z) = z - b$ as a function from $G$ to $\mathbb{C}$. Note that $h$ is a nowhere-zero holomorphic function in $G$. Hence, there exists a function $g: G \to \mathbb{C}$ with $h = g^2$.\retTwo

	Claim 1: $g$ is injective.
	\begin{myIndent}\exPPP
		Suppose $g(z_1) = g(z_2)$. Then $z_1 - b = g(z_1)^2 = g(z_2)^2 = z_2 - b \Longrightarrow z_1 = z_2$.\retTwo
	\end{myIndent}

	Claim 2: $g(G) \cap (-g)(G) = \emptyset$.
	\begin{myIndent}\exPPP
		Suppose for the sake of contradiction that there exists $z_1, z_2 \in G$ with $g(z_1) = -g(z_2)$. Then $g(z_1)^2 = g(z_2)^2 \Longrightarrow z_1 = z_2$. So, we've shown that $g(z_1) = -g(z_1)$. That in turn implies that $g(z_1)^2 = 0$. So, $h(z_1) = g(z_1)^2 = 0$. But now we have a contradiction as $h \neq 0$ everywhere on $G$.\retTwo
	\end{myIndent}

	Claim 3: There exists $c \in \mathbb{C}$ and $r > 0$ such that $|g(z) - c| > r$ on $G$.
	\begin{myIndent}\exPPP
		By the open mapping theorem, we know that $(-g)(G)$ is an open set in $\mathbb{C}$. So, $\exists \overline{\Delta}(c, r) \subseteq (-g)(G)$. In turn, by claim 2 we have that $g(G) \cap \overline{\Delta}(c, r) = \emptyset$.\retTwo
	\end{myIndent}

	Let $\widetilde{f}(z) = \frac{r}{g(z) - c}$. By claim 3 we know that $\widetilde{f}$ mapso $G$ into $\Delta$. Also because $g$ is injective, we know that $\widetilde{f}$ is as well.\newpage

	Finally, set $f(z) = \frac{1}{2}(\widetilde{f}(z) - \widetilde{f}(a))$. By the convexity of $\Delta$, we know that $f$ maps $G$ into $\Delta$. Also $f$ is injective because $\widetilde{f}$ is. And finally, $f(a) = 0$.\retTwo

	\begin{myIndent}\myComment
		Our next goal will be to find a function $f \in \mathcal{F}$ with a maximal value for $|f^\prime(a)|$. For some intuition on why'd we want to do this, note that if $f \in \mathcal{F}$ is to be surjective, then we'll need $f$ to expand out each "infinitesimally small" patch of area as much as possible, and from a basic calculus class we know that expansion is proportional to the derivative of $f$.\retTwo
	\end{myIndent}

	Set $\mathcal{F} \coloneqq \{f : G \to \Delta : f \text{ is injective, holomorphic, and }f(a) = 0\}$. Then set\\ $M  = \sup_{f \in \mathcal{F}}|f^\prime(a)|$. (As we will show, $M$ is finite. That said, for now we will allow\\ for $M$ to be infinite.)\retTwo

	We can find a sequence $\{f_n\}_{n \in \mathbb{N}}$ with $|f_n^\prime(a)| \to M$ as $n \to \infty$. But note that $\mathcal{F}$ is\\ [1pt] bounded by $1$ on $G$. Therefore, by Montel's theorem we have that $\mathcal{F}$ is normal. In turn, by\\ [-1pt] passing to a subsequence we may assume that $f_n \locUConverges f$ for some $f \in O(G)$.\retTwo

	Note that $f_n(a) = 0$ for all $n$ implies that $f(a) = 0$. Also by the Weierstraß convergence theorem, we know that $f^\prime_n(a) \to f^\prime(a)$ as $n \to \infty$. This proves that $|f^\prime(a)| = M$. (which also shows $M$ is finite). Thirdly, by our second corollary to Hurwitz's theorem (see \inLinkRap{Hurwitz' theorem corollary 2}{page 530}) we know that either $f$ is injective or $f$ is constant. But by the following reasoning, we can conclude $f$ is not constant. So, $f$ must be injective.
	\begin{myIndent}\exPPP
		If $f$ is constant, then we must have that $f^\prime(a) = 0$. Yet that would imply $M = 0$ and therefore $g^\prime(a) = 0$ for all $g \in \mathcal{F}$. This contradicts that each $g \in \mathcal{F}$ is injective.\retTwo
	\end{myIndent}

	Fourthly, since $f$ is injective (and thus nonconstant), we know by the open mapping\\ theorem that $f(G)$ is open. Also, as $f_n(z) \in \Delta$ for all $n \in \mathbb{N}$ and $z \in G$, we can\\ conclude that $f(G) \subseteq \overline{\Delta}$. Putting the prior two observations together, we have that $f(G) \subseteq (\overline{\Delta})^\circ = \Delta$. So, $f$ maps $G$ into $\Delta$. This proves that $f \in \mathcal{F}$.\retTwo

	\begin{myIndent}\myComment
		Our third and final goal will be to show that if $f \in \mathcal{F}$ satisfies that $|f^\prime(a)|$ is maximal, then $f$ is a surjection.\retTwo
	\end{myIndent}

	% Let $f \in \mathcal{F}$ satisfy that $|f^\prime(a)| = M$ and suppose for the sake of contradiction that\\ $f(G) \neq \Delta$. In that case, we can find some $w \in \Delta$ with $w \notin f(G)$. In turn, by composing the biholomorphism $\varphi_w(z) = \frac{z - w}{1 - \overline{w}z}$ of the unit disk with $f$, we can see that\\ [-3pt] $(\varphi_{w} \circ f)(z) = \frac{f(z) - w}{1 - \overline{w}f(z)}$ is a holomorphic function from $G$ to $\Delta$ that vanishes\\ [-2pt] nowhere on $G$.\retTwo

	% Next, using once again that $G$ is a root domain, we know there exists a holomorphic\\ function $h \in O(G)$ with $h^2(z) = \frac{f(z) - w}{1 - \overline{w}f(z)}$. Since $|h(z)| = |f(z)|^{1/2} < 1$ we know that $h$ maps $G$ into $\Delta$.
\end{myIndent}

\Hstatement\blab{Set 6 Problem 5:} Let $G \subseteq \mathbb{C}$ be a simply connected region with $G \neq \mathbb{C}$.
\begin{enumerate}
	\item[(a)] Show that any holomorphic map $f: G \to G$ other than the identity admits at most one\\ fixed point.
	\begin{myIndent}\HexOne
		Suppose for the sake of contradiction that $f$ has two distinct fixed points $a, b \in G$. By\\ the Riemann mapping theorem, we know there is a biholomorphism $\theta: G \to \Delta$. In\\ turn, we know that $g \coloneqq \theta \circ f \circ \theta^{-1}$ is a holomorphic function from $\Delta$ to $\Delta$. Also,\\ $g \coloneqq \theta \circ f \circ \theta^{-1}$ has two distinct fixed points $\alpha \coloneqq \theta(a)$ and $\beta \coloneqq \theta(b)$. If we can prove that $g$ is the identity map, then we will also know that $f$ is the identity map.\retTwo

		Given any $z_0 \in \Delta$, let $\varphi_{z_0}(z)$ denote the biholomorphism of the form $\frac{z - z_0}{1 - \overline{z_0} z}$. Then note that $h \coloneqq \varphi_{\alpha} \circ g \circ \varphi_{-\alpha}$ is a holomorphism of $\Delta$ with the distinct fixed points $0$ and $\varphi_\alpha(\beta)$. By Schwarz's lemma, we can thus conclude that $h$ is a rotation. Yet the only rotation with a fixed point besides $0$ is the identity map. So, $h$ is the identity map. In turn, so is $g$.
	\end{myIndent}
	
	\item[(b)] Show that the above statement is false for $G = \mathbb{C}$.
	\begin{myIndent}\HexOne
		Let $f(z) = z^2$. Then $f$ is a holomorphic map from $\mathbb{C}$ to $\mathbb{C}$ with $0$ and $1$ as fixed points. This contradicts part (a).
	\end{myIndent}
	
	\item[(c)] Now assume $f: G \to G$ is holomorphic and admits a fixed point $a$ such that $f^\prime(a) = 1$.\\ Show that $f$ must be the identity.
	\begin{myIndent}\HexOne
		Let $\theta: G \to \Delta$ be a biholomorphic function sending $a$ to $0$. Then note that \\$g = \theta \circ f \circ \theta^{-1}$ is a holomorphic function from $\Delta$ to $\Delta$ such that $g(0) = 0$.\\ Also note that:
		
		{\centering\begin{tabular}{l}
			$g^\prime(0) = \theta^\prime(f(\theta^{-1}(0))) \cdot f^\prime(\theta^{-1}(0)) \cdot (\theta^{-1})^\prime(0)$\\ [6pt]
			$\phantom{g^\prime(0)} = \theta^\prime(a) \cdot f^\prime(a) \cdot (\theta^{-1})^\prime(0) = \theta^\prime(a) \cdot f^\prime(a) \cdot \frac{1}{\theta^\prime(\theta^{-1}(0))} = \frac{\theta^\prime(a)}{\theta^\prime(a)} \cdot f^\prime(a) = 1$
		\end{tabular}\retTwo\par}

		It follows by Schwarz' lemma that $g$ is a rotation. Also, since the only rotation with $g^\prime(0) = 1$ is the identity map, we know that $g$ is the identity map. This in turn proves that $f$ is also the identity map. $\blacksquare$\retTwo
	\end{myIndent}
\end{enumerate}
